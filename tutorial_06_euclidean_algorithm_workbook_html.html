How to use this workbook

The Euclidean algorithm is a repeated division process. At each step, divide the larger number by the smaller number, record the remainder, then replace the pair with the divisor and the remainder. Stop when the remainder becomes 0. The answer is the last nonzero remainder.

Note for self-study

Always check that each remainder is smaller than the previous divisor. The answer is the last nonzero remainder, not the zero remainder.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

A1. \(\gcd(a,b)\) is the largest number that divides both \(a\) and \(b\) evenly.

A2. \(q\) is the quotient, or how many full copies fit. \(r\) is the remainder, or what is left over.

A3. The next pair is \((b,r)\).

A4. \(\gcd(a,n)=1\) means \(a\) and \(n\) are coprime. That is exactly when \(a\) has a multiplicative inverse modulo \(n\).

Part G: Explain the process

G1 sample answer. If \(a=qb+r\), then \(r=a-qb\). Any number that divides both \(a\) and \(b\) also divides the remainder \(r\). So the common divisor problem does not change when we replace \((a,b)\) with \((b,r)\).

G2 sample answer. The algorithm stops when the remainder is 0, meaning the previous divisor divides evenly. The last nonzero remainder is the largest value that divides the original pair.

G3 sample answer. The Euclidean algorithm finds the greatest common divisor. The extended Euclidean algorithm also finds coefficients that express the greatest common divisor as a combination of the original two numbers, which lets us calculate modular inverses.

H1.

\[ 987=1\cdot610+377 \] \[ 610=1\cdot377+233 \] \[ 377=1\cdot233+144 \] \[ 233=1\cdot144+89 \] \[ 144=1\cdot89+55 \] \[ 89=1\cdot55+34 \] \[ 55=1\cdot34+21 \] \[ 34=1\cdot21+13 \] \[ 21=1\cdot13+8 \] \[ 13=1\cdot8+5 \] \[ 8=1\cdot5+3 \] \[ 5=1\cdot3+2 \] \[ 3=1\cdot2+1 \] \[ 2=2\cdot1+0 \]

\(\gcd(987,610)=1\).

H2.

\[ 4096=16\cdot255+16 \] \[ 255=15\cdot16+15 \] \[ 16=1\cdot15+1 \] \[ 15=15\cdot1+0 \]

\(\gcd(4096,255)=1\). Therefore 255 has an inverse modulo 4096.
