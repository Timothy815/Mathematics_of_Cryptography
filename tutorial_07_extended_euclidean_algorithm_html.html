The Extended Euclidean Algorithm

Finding the hidden coefficients inside the greatest common divisor, then using them to compute modular inverses.

← Tutorial 6 🏠 Dashboard Tutorial 8 →

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

The Euclidean algorithm tells us the greatest common divisor. That is enough to decide whether a modular inverse exists. But cryptography often needs the inverse itself, not merely permission to have one. The extended Euclidean algorithm gives us that missing machinery.

The ordinary Euclidean algorithm walks forward until it finds the greatest common divisor. The extended version then walks backward, rewriting that greatest common divisor in terms of the original two numbers. Those hidden coefficients are the treasure map to modular inverses.

What you should know first

You should understand division with remainder, the Euclidean algorithm, greatest common divisors, congruence, and the rule that \(a^{-1}\pmod n\) exists exactly when \(\gcd(a,n)=1\).

Symbols used in this tutorial

This lesson introduces coefficients. They are not decorative algebra confetti; they are the numbers that let us reverse modular multiplication.

Symbol or notation Friendly meaning Technical meaning
\(a\), \(b\) The two starting numbers. Integers, usually positive in this tutorial.
\(\gcd(a,b)\) The largest number that divides both starting numbers. The greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\).
\(q\) How many full copies fit in a division step. The quotient in division with remainder.
\(r\) The leftover amount in a division step. The remainder in division with remainder.
\(s\), \(t\) The hidden multipliers attached to the original numbers. Integer coefficients in Bézout's identity.

\(a^{-1}\pmod n\) The number that undoes multiplication by \(a\) modulo \(n\). A residue \(x\) such that \(ax\equiv1\pmod n\).

The big idea

Intuition first

The ordinary Euclidean algorithm says, “Here is the greatest common divisor.” The extended Euclidean algorithm says, “Here is how to build that divisor using the original numbers.” When the divisor is 1, that recipe becomes a modular inverse.

Bézout's identity

Definition: Bézout's identity

For integers \(a\) and \(b\), there exist integers \(s\) and \(t\) such that the greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) can be written as a linear combination of \(a\) and \(b\).

\[ \gcd(a,b)=sa+tb \]

The numbers \(s\) and \(t\) are called Bézout coefficients. They can be positive, negative, or zero. The extended Euclidean algorithm finds them.

Why this finds modular inverses

Suppose we want the inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\). If \(a\) and \(n\) are coprime, then:

\[ \gcd(a,n)=1 \]

Bézout's identity says there are integers \(s\) and \(t\) such that:

\[ 1=sa+tn \]

Now reduce both sides modulo \(n\). The term \(tn\) disappears because it is a multiple of \(n\):

\[ 1\equiv sa\pmod n \]

That means \(s\) is the inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\).

\[ a^{-1}\equiv s\pmod n \]
The treasure-map sentence

If you can rewrite 1 as “some copies of \(a\) plus some copies of \(n\),” then the coefficient on \(a\) is the modular inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\).

Worked example 1: find \(25^{-1}\pmod{151}\)

We want a number \(x\) such that:

\[ 25x\equiv1\pmod{151} \]

Step 1: Run the Euclidean algorithm

\[ 151=6\cdot25+1 \]
\[ 25=25\cdot1+0 \]

The greatest common divisor is 1, so an inverse exists.

Step 2: Solve the nonzero remainder equation for 1

From the first equation:

\[ 151=6\cdot25+1 \]

Rearrange it to isolate 1:

\[ 1=151-6\cdot25 \]

Step 3: Read the coefficient of 25

The coefficient of 25 is \(-6\). Therefore:

\[ 25^{-1}\equiv -6\pmod{151} \]

Convert \(-6\) to a standard positive residue by adding 151:

\[ -6+151=145 \]

So:

\[ 25^{-1}\equiv145\pmod{151} \]

Step 4: Check

\[ 25\cdot145=3625 \] \[ 3625\bmod151=1 \]

This example takes several back-substitution steps, so we will move slowly.

We have written \(1\) as a combination of 15 and 26: \[ 1=7\cdot15-4\cdot26 \]

Step 4: Read the inverse

The coefficient of 15 is 7, so:

\[ 15^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{26} \]

Step 5: Check

\[ 15\cdot7=105 \] \[ 105\bmod26=1 \]

Negative coefficients are normal

During back-substitution, coefficients often become negative. That is not a mistake. Modular arithmetic can convert a negative coefficient into its standard positive residue.

Converting a negative inverse

If the coefficient is negative, add the modulus until it lands in the standard range \(0\) through \(n-1\).

Example

If the coefficient of \(a\) is \(-6\) modulo 151, then:

\[ -6\equiv145\pmod{151} \]

So \(-6\) and 145 represent the same residue modulo 151.

The standard table method

Back-substitution is conceptually clear but can get messy with many steps. The standard table method eliminates the algebra by tracking everything mechanically. It has seven columns.

Column What it holds
\(n\)The current larger number — starts as the modulus, shifts down each row.
\(b\)The current smaller number — starts as the value to invert, shifts down each row.
\(q\)The quotient: \(q = \lfloor n \div b \rfloor\).
\(r\)The remainder: \(r = n - q \cdot b\).
\(t_1\)The coefficient from two rows back (first row: 0).
\(t_2\)The coefficient from one row back (first row: 1).
\(t_3\)The new coefficient: \(t_3 = t_1 - q \cdot t_2\).
Initialization and the recurrence

Start with \(t_1 = 0\) and \(t_2 = 1\). At every row, compute \(t_3 = t_1 - q \cdot t_2\). Then shift: \(n \leftarrow b\), \(b \leftarrow r\), \(t_1 \leftarrow t_2\), \(t_2 \leftarrow t_3\). Stop when \(r = 1\). The value of \(t_3\) in that row is the modular inverse.

Example: find \(15^{-1}\pmod{26}\)

\(n\) \(b\) \(q\) \(r = n - q \cdot b\) \(t_1\) \(t_2\) \(t_3 = t_1 - q \cdot t_2\)
2615111 01\(0 - 1\cdot1 = -1\)
151114 1−1\(1 - 1\cdot(-1) = 2\)
11423 −12\(-1 - 2\cdot2 = -5\)
4311 2−5\(2 - 1\cdot(-5) = \mathbf{7}\)

The remainder reached 1 in row 4. The value of \(t_3\) in that row is 7, so:

\[ 15^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{26} \]

Example: find \(25^{-1}\pmod{151}\)

This one finishes in a single step.

\(n\) \(b\) \(q\) \(r = n - q \cdot b\) \(t_1\) \(t_2\) \(t_3 = t_1 - q \cdot t_2\)
1512561 01\(0 - 6\cdot1 = \mathbf{-6}\)

The remainder hit 1 immediately, but \(t_3 = -6\). Since the answer must be a positive residue, add the modulus:

\[ -6 + 151 = 145 \]
\[ 25^{-1}\equiv145\pmod{151} \]
Why the t column works

At every row, \(t_3\) is secretly the coefficient of \(b\) when the remainder is written as a linear combination of the two original numbers. The recurrence \(t_3 = t_1 - q \cdot t_2\) mirrors exactly the arithmetic of back-substitution, but computes it forward row by row instead of walking backward at the end. When the remainder equals 1, the coefficient of \(b\) in that combination is the modular inverse.

When \(t_3\) is negative, simply add the modulus \(n\) once to shift it into the standard range \(\{0, \ldots, n-1\}\). Adding \(n\) does not change the modular value (since \(n \equiv 0 \pmod n\)), so the inverse is unaffected.

What happens when the gcd is not 1?

If \(\gcd(a,n)\neq1\), then \(a\) has no inverse modulo \(n\). The extended Euclidean algorithm can still express the greatest common divisor as a combination of \(a\) and \(n\), but it will not produce an inverse.

Example: 13 modulo 26

\[ 26=2\cdot13+0 \]

The greatest common divisor is 13, not 1. Since we cannot write 1 as a combination of 13 and 26, there is no inverse of 13 modulo 26.

Important boundary

The extended Euclidean algorithm does not magically make non-invertible numbers invertible. It reveals whether the door exists, and if the door exists, it gives you the key.

Cryptography example: affine cipher decryption

The affine cipher encrypts with:

\[ C\equiv aP+b\pmod{26} \]

To decrypt, we use:

\[ P\equiv a^{-1}(C-b)\pmod{26} \]

Example

Suppose:

\[ C\equiv15P+8\pmod{26} \]

We found:

\[ 15^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{26} \]

If \(C=13\), then:

\[ P\equiv7(13-8)\pmod{26} \] \[ P\equiv7\cdot5\pmod{26} \] \[ P\equiv35\pmod{26} \] \[ P\equiv9\pmod{26} \]

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: losing signs during back-substitution

When substituting an expression inside parentheses, distribute the negative sign carefully. Many wrong inverses are born from one escaped minus sign skittering across the page.

Mistake 2: reading the wrong coefficient

To find \(a^{-1}\pmod n\), use the coefficient of \(a\), not the coefficient of \(n\).

Mistake 3: forgetting to reduce negative answers

If the coefficient is negative, convert it to a standard residue by adding the modulus. For example, \(-6\equiv145\pmod{151}\).

Mistake 4: expecting an inverse when the gcd is not 1

If \(\gcd(a,n)\neq1\), then \(a^{-1}\pmod n\) does not exist.

Practice problems

  1. Use the extended Euclidean algorithm to find \(7^{-1}\pmod{12}\).
    Show solution
    \[ 12=1\cdot7+5 \] \[ 7=1\cdot5+2 \] \[ 5=2\cdot2+1 \]

    Back-substitute:

    \[ 1=5-2\cdot2 \] \[ 1=5-2(7-5)=3\cdot5-2\cdot7 \] \[ 1=3(12-7)-2\cdot7=3\cdot12-5\cdot7 \]

    The coefficient of 7 is \(-5\). Since \(-5\equiv7\pmod{12}\), \(7^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{12}\).

  2. Use the extended Euclidean algorithm to find \(11^{-1}\pmod{26}\).
    Show solution
    \[ 26=2\cdot11+4 \] \[ 11=2\cdot4+3 \] \[ 4=1\cdot3+1 \]

    Back-substitute:

    \[ 1=4-3 \] \[ 1=4-(11-2\cdot4)=3\cdot4-11 \] \[ 1=3(26-2\cdot11)-11=3\cdot26-7\cdot11 \]

    The coefficient of 11 is \(-7\). Since \(-7\equiv19\pmod{26}\), \(11^{-1}\equiv19\pmod{26}\).

  3. Use the extended Euclidean algorithm to find \(35^{-1}\pmod{64}\).
    Show solution
    \[ 64=1\cdot35+29 \] \[ 35=1\cdot29+6 \] \[ 29=4\cdot6+5 \] \[ 6=1\cdot5+1 \]

    Back-substitute:

    \[ 1=6-5 \] \[ 1=6-(29-4\cdot6)=5\cdot6-29 \] \[ 1=5(35-29)-29=5\cdot35-6\cdot29 \] \[ 1=5\cdot35-6(64-35)=11\cdot35-6\cdot64 \]

    The coefficient of 35 is 11, so \(35^{-1}\equiv11\pmod{64}\).

Why this matters for cryptography

The extended Euclidean algorithm is one of the most practical tools in computational number theory.

  • It computes modular inverses efficiently.
  • It supports affine cipher decryption.
  • It is used in RSA key generation to compute private exponents.
  • It appears in elliptic curve cryptography when dividing by field elements.
  • The polynomial version later helps explain finite-field inverses.

One-page recap

What to remember
  • The Euclidean algorithm finds \(\gcd(a,b)\).
  • The extended Euclidean algorithm finds coefficients \(s\) and \(t\).
  • Bézout's identity says \(\gcd(a,b)=sa+tb\).
  • If \(\gcd(a,n)=1\), then \(1=sa+tn\).
  • Reducing modulo \(n\), the \(tn\) term disappears.
  • The coefficient \(s\) of \(a\) is the inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\).
  • If \(s\) is negative, add \(n\) until it is in the standard range.
  • If the gcd is not 1, no modular inverse exists.
