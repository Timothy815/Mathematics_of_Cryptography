How to use this workbook

Work in two phases. First, run the Euclidean algorithm forward to find the greatest common divisor. Second, walk backward through the remainder equations to express that greatest common divisor as a combination of the original two numbers.

Note for self-study

In inverse problems, always read the coefficient of the number being inverted, then reduce that coefficient modulo the modulus.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

A1. Bézout's identity says the greatest common divisor of two integers can be written as an integer combination of those numbers: \(\gcd(a,b)=sa+tb\).

A2. Bézout coefficients are the integers \(s\) and \(t\) that multiply the original numbers in that combination.

A3. If \(1=sa+tn\), then modulo \(n\), the \(tn\) term disappears, leaving \(1\equiv sa\pmod n\). So \(s\) is the inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\).

A4. Add the modulus until the coefficient lands in the standard range \(0\) through \(n-1\).

Part B: Forward Euclidean steps

B1.

\[ 26=1\cdot15+11 \] \[ 15=1\cdot11+4 \] \[ 11=2\cdot4+3 \] \[ 4=1\cdot3+1 \] \[ 3=3\cdot1+0 \]

\(\gcd(26,15)=1\).

B2.

\[ 151=6\cdot25+1 \] \[ 25=25\cdot1+0 \]

\(\gcd(151,25)=1\).

Part C: Guided back-substitution

C1.

\[ 1=4-3 \] \[ 1=4-(11-2\cdot4)=3\cdot4-11 \] \[ 1=3(15-11)-11=3\cdot15-4\cdot11 \] \[ 1=3\cdot15-4(26-15)=7\cdot15-4\cdot26 \]

So \(15^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{26}\).

C2.

\[ 151=6\cdot25+1 \] \[ 1=151-6\cdot25 \]

The coefficient of 25 is \(-6\). Since \(-6\equiv145\pmod{151}\), \(25^{-1}\equiv145\pmod{151}\).

Part D: Find modular inverses

D1.

\[ 12=1\cdot7+5 \] \[ 7=1\cdot5+2 \] \[ 5=2\cdot2+1 \] \[ 1=5-2\cdot2 \] \[ 1=5-2(7-5)=3\cdot5-2\cdot7 \] \[ 1=3(12-7)-2\cdot7=3\cdot12-5\cdot7 \]

The coefficient of 7 is \(-5\), and \(-5\equiv7\pmod{12}\). So \(7^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{12}\).

D2.

\[ 26=2\cdot11+4 \] \[ 11=2\cdot4+3 \] \[ 4=1\cdot3+1 \] \[ 1=4-3 \] \[ 1=4-(11-2\cdot4)=3\cdot4-11 \] \[ 1=3(26-2\cdot11)-11=3\cdot26-7\cdot11 \]

The coefficient of 11 is \(-7\), and \(-7\equiv19\pmod{26}\). So \(11^{-1}\equiv19\pmod{26}\).

D3.

\[ 64=1\cdot35+29 \] \[ 35=1\cdot29+6 \] \[ 29=4\cdot6+5 \] \[ 6=1\cdot5+1 \] \[ 1=6-5 \] \[ 1=6-(29-4\cdot6)=5\cdot6-29 \] \[ 1=5(35-29)-29=5\cdot35-6\cdot29 \] \[ 1=5\cdot35-6(64-35)=11\cdot35-6\cdot64 \]

So \(35^{-1}\equiv11\pmod{64}\).

Part G: Non-invertible cases

G1 sample answer. \(13^{-1}\pmod{26}\) does not exist because \(\gcd(13,26)=13\), not 1. Since 13 and 26 are not coprime, 13 has no modular inverse modulo 26.

G2 sample answer. To find an inverse, we need to rewrite 1 as \(sa+tn\). If the greatest common divisor is not 1, the extended Euclidean algorithm rewrites that larger greatest common divisor instead, so it cannot produce an inverse.

Part I: Challenge problem

I1.

\[ 3120=183\cdot17+9 \] \[ 17=1\cdot9+8 \] \[ 9=1\cdot8+1 \]

Back-substitute:

\[ 1=9-8 \] \[ 1=9-(17-9)=2\cdot9-17 \] \[ 1=2(3120-183\cdot17)-17 \] \[ 1=2\cdot3120-367\cdot17 \]

The coefficient of 17 is \(-367\). Convert it modulo 3120:

\[ -367+3120=2753 \]

So \(17^{-1}\equiv2753\pmod{3120}\).
