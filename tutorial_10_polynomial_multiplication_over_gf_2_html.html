Polynomial Multiplication over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\)

Multiplying byte-polynomials before reduction: distribute terms, add exponents, and cancel duplicate powers modulo 2.

← Module 09 🏠 Dashboard Module 11 →

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

In Tutorial 9, we learned that adding byte-polynomials over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) is XOR. Now we learn how to multiply those polynomials. This is the last major step before polynomial reduction, where high powers of \(x\) will be folded back into byte-sized form.

Polynomial multiplication over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) looks like ordinary polynomial multiplication at first: distribute every term against every term. The twist comes when like powers combine: matching terms cancel in pairs because coefficients are added modulo 2.

What you should know first

You should know how to read bytes as polynomials, how coefficient addition over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) works, and why duplicate terms cancel. You do not need polynomial reduction yet. This tutorial intentionally stops before AES's folding rule.

Learning objectives

After completing this module you will be able to
  • Apply the rule \(x^i\cdot x^j = x^{i+j}\) to multiply any two single terms.
  • Distribute every term of one polynomial across every term of another and list all partial products.
  • Combine like powers over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) by cancelling duplicate terms in pairs.
  • Compute the unreduced product of any two byte-polynomials, including cases where the degree exceeds 7.
  • Explain why polynomial multiplication may produce powers above \(x^7\) and why reduction will be needed next.
  • Distinguish polynomial multiplication over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) from ordinary integer polynomial multiplication.

The big idea

Intuition first

Multiplying polynomials is a pairing process. Every term in the first polynomial shakes hands with every term in the second polynomial. Each handshake creates a new power of \(x\). Then matching powers cancel in pairs if they appear an even number of times.

Multiplying single terms

The basic rule is the same exponent rule you may remember from algebra:

\[ x^i\cdot x^j=x^{i+j} \]
Multiplication Exponent addition Result
\(x^3\cdot x^2\)\(3+2=5\)\(x^5\)
\(x^7\cdot x\)\(7+1=8\)\(x^8\)
\(x^4\cdot1\)\(4+0=4\)\(x^4\)
\(1\cdot1\)\(0+0=0\)\(1\)
Why \(1\) acts like \(x^0\)

The constant term \(1\) can be thought of as \(x^0\). That is why \(x^4\cdot1=x^4\cdot x^0=x^4\).

Coefficient multiplication over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\)

Coefficients are still bits, so multiplying coefficients is simple.

In practice, a term only contributes to the product if it appears in both selected positions. A 0 coefficient is like a silent switch: it contributes nothing.

Worked example 1: multiply two small polynomials

Multiply:

\[ (x+1)(x^2+x+1) \]

Step 1: Distribute every term

\[ x(x^2+x+1)+1(x^2+x+1) \] \[ x^3+x^2+x+x^2+x+1 \]

Step 2: Cancel duplicates over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\)

The \(x^2\) terms appear twice, so they cancel. The \(x\) terms also appear twice, so they cancel.

\[ x^3+\cancel{x^2}+\cancel{x}+\cancel{x^2}+\cancel{x}+1=x^3+1 \]
Result
\[ (x+1)(x^2+x+1)=x^3+1\quad\text{over }\mathrm{GF}(2) \]

Worked example 2: grid view

Multiply:

\[ (x^2+x+1)(x^3+x) \]

Pair every term in the first polynomial with every term in the second polynomial:

\(x^2\cdot x^3=x^5\)
\(x^2\cdot x=x^3\)
\(x\cdot x^3=x^4\)
\(x\cdot x=x^2\)
\(1\cdot x^3=x^3\)
\(1\cdot x=x\)

Write all terms:

\[ x^5+x^3+x^4+x^2+x^3+x \]

Now combine like terms. The \(x^3\) terms appear twice, so they cancel:

\[ x^5+x^4+x^2+x \]

Worked example 3: multiplying byte-polynomials

Let us multiply two familiar byte-polynomials, but remember: this is still the unreduced product.

\[ \texttt{0x57}=x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \] \[ \texttt{0x03}=x+1 \]

Multiply:

\[ (x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)(x+1) \]

Step 1: Multiply by \(x\)

\[ x(x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)=x^7+x^5+x^3+x^2+x \]

Step 2: Multiply by \(1\)

\[ 1(x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)=x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \]

Step 3: Add the two partial products over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\)

\[ (x^7+x^5+x^3+x^2+x)+(x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1) \]

The \(x^2\) terms cancel, and the \(x\) terms cancel.

\[ x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+1 \]
Important boundary

This is the unreduced polynomial product. It happens to fit within degree 7, but many products will create \(x^8\), \(x^9\), or higher. Reducing those high powers back into byte form comes in the next tutorial.

Why high powers appear

When multiplying byte-polynomials, the highest possible term in each byte is \(x^7\). If two \(x^7\) terms multiply, the result is:

\[ x^7\cdot x^7=x^{14} \]

That is much larger than a byte-polynomial, which only has powers \(x^7\) down to \(1\). This is why polynomial multiplication alone is not yet \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\) multiplication.

The next problem

Multiplication can push us above byte size. The next tutorial will explain the folding rule that brings high powers back down.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: forgetting to distribute every term

Every term in the first polynomial must multiply every term in the second polynomial. Missing one pairing changes the product.

Mistake 2: combining like terms using ordinary coefficients

Over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), \(x^3+x^3=0\), not \(2x^3\).

Mistake 3: reducing too early

This tutorial stops at the unreduced product. Do not fold high powers yet. That rule requires an irreducible polynomial, which comes next.

Mistake 4: treating \(x\) like the number 2

We are not evaluating the polynomial at \(x=2\). The symbol \(x\) remains a formal polynomial placeholder.

Practice problems

  1. Multiply \((x+1)(x+1)\) over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).
    Show solution
    \[ (x+1)(x+1)=x^2+x+x+1=x^2+1 \]

    The two \(x\) terms cancel.

  2. Multiply \((x+1)(x^2+x+1)\) over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).
    Show solution
    \[ (x+1)(x^2+x+1)=x^3+x^2+x+x^2+x+1=x^3+1 \]
  3. Multiply \((x^2+x+1)(x^3+x)\) over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).
    Show solution
    \[ x^2(x^3+x)+x(x^3+x)+1(x^3+x) \] \[ =x^5+x^3+x^4+x^2+x^3+x \] \[ =x^5+x^4+x^2+x \]
  4. Multiply \((x^7+1)(x+1)\) over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\). Leave the result unreduced.
    Show solution
    \[ (x^7+1)(x+1)=x^8+x^7+x+1 \]
  5. Multiply \((x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)(x+1)\) over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).
    Show solution
    \[ x(x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)=x^7+x^5+x^3+x^2+x \] \[ 1(x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)=x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \] \[ \text{sum}=x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+1 \]

Why this matters for cryptography

Polynomial multiplication over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) is the engine underneath byte multiplication in finite fields.

  • AES treats bytes as polynomials with binary coefficients.
  • Adding partial products uses XOR, not ordinary integer addition.
  • Multiplication can create powers higher than \(x^7\).
  • Those high powers must be reduced by a special polynomial rule to stay byte-sized.
  • This prepares us for multiplication in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\).

One-page recap

What to remember
  • Multiply powers by adding exponents: \(x^i\cdot x^j=x^{i+j}\).
  • Distribute every term in one polynomial across every term in the other.
  • Coefficient multiplication uses 0 and 1.
  • Combine like terms by adding coefficients modulo 2.
  • Duplicate terms cancel in pairs.
  • The result of multiplication may have powers higher than \(x^7\).
  • This tutorial gives the unreduced product only.
  • Reduction comes next.
Rule Example
Multiply powers by adding exponents \(x^3\cdot x^2=x^5\)
Duplicate terms cancel \(x^4+x^4=0\)
Distribute terms \((x+1)(x^2+1)=x^3+x^2+x+1\)
High powers may appear \(x^7\cdot x^7=x^{14}\)
Core example
