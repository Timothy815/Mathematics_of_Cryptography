How to use this workbook

Multiply first, then simplify. Use \(x^i\cdot x^j=x^{i+j}\). Distribute every term. Combine like powers by adding coefficients modulo 2, which means duplicate terms cancel in pairs. Do not reduce high powers yet.

Quick reference

Core rules
RuleExample
Multiply powers by adding exponents\(x^3\cdot x^2=x^5\)
Multiply by \(1\)\(x^4\cdot1=x^4\)
Duplicate terms cancel\(x^4+x^4=0\)
Leave high powers unreduced\(x^7\cdot x^7=x^{14}\)
Process
  1. Distribute every term.
  2. Add exponents when multiplying powers of \(x\).
  3. List all generated terms.
  4. Cancel pairs of matching terms.
  5. Leave the result unreduced.

Note for self-study

Keep the two phases separate: first generate all product terms, then simplify by canceling duplicate terms. Do not reduce high powers yet.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

A1. To distribute means to multiply every term in one polynomial by every term in the other polynomial.

A2. When multiplying powers of \(x\), keep the same base and add the exponents.

A3. Duplicate terms cancel because their coefficients add as \(1+1=0\) over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).

A4. An unreduced product is the polynomial result before high powers are folded back down by a reduction rule.

Part H: Concept checks

H1 sample answer. Addition only combines existing powers, so it cannot create a new higher power. Multiplication adds exponents, so \(x^7\cdot x^7\) creates \(x^{14}\).

H2 sample answer. We are not reducing high powers yet because reduction requires a chosen polynomial rule. The AES rule comes from an irreducible polynomial, which belongs in the next lesson.

H3 sample answer. Ordinary polynomial multiplication distributes terms and combines like powers using normal integer coefficients. Over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), the distribution is similar, but like-term coefficients are reduced modulo 2, so duplicate terms cancel.
