Polynomial Reduction and Irreducible Polynomials

Folding high powers back into byte-sized form: the polynomial version of modular wraparound.

← Module 10 🏠 Dashboard Module 12 →

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

Polynomial multiplication can create powers like \(x^8\), \(x^9\), or even \(x^{14}\). But a byte-polynomial can only use powers from \(x^7\) down to \(1\). This tutorial explains how high powers are reduced, or folded, back into byte-sized form.

Integer modular arithmetic has a wraparound rule. Polynomial finite-field arithmetic has a folding rule. Instead of reducing a number modulo an integer, we reduce a polynomial modulo another polynomial. This is the moment where the clock becomes a little algebraic machine.

What you should know first

You should know how to read bytes as polynomials, how addition over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) works, and how to multiply polynomials over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) before reduction. You should also remember the idea of a remainder from modular arithmetic.

Learning objectives

After completing this module you will be able to
  • Explain why polynomial products must be reduced and what "degree" means in this context.
  • Derive a folding rule from any modulus polynomial by rearranging the congruence \(m(x)\equiv0\).
  • Perform polynomial reduction step-by-step with both a toy modulus and the AES modulus.
  • Identify the AES modulus \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\) and explain why \(\texttt{0x1B}\) appears when folding \(x^8\).
  • Explain what "irreducible" means and why it matters for building a finite field.

The big idea

Intuition first

Reduction is controlled substitution. If the modulus polynomial tells us that a high power of \(x\) is equivalent to a smaller combination of terms, then we replace the high power with that smaller combination. Repeat until every term fits below the modulus degree.

The integer modular arithmetic analogy

In integer modular arithmetic, reducing modulo \(n\) means keeping the remainder after division by \(n\):

\[ 29\bmod 12=5 \]

Because:

\[ 29=2\cdot12+5 \]

Polynomial reduction works the same way in spirit:

\[ p(x)=q(x)m(x)+r(x) \]

The reduced result is the remainder \(r(x)\), whose degree is smaller than the degree of \(m(x)\).

Definition: polynomial reduction

Polynomial reduction modulo \(m(x)\) means finding the remainder after dividing by \(m(x)\). The result must have degree smaller than \(m(x)\).

A small toy modulus first

Before AES's degree-8 rule, use a smaller rule so the idea is visible. Let:

\[ m(x)=x^3+x+1 \]

Since we reduce modulo \(m(x)\), we treat \(m(x)\) as equivalent to 0:

\[ x^3+x+1\equiv0\pmod{m(x)} \]

Rearranging over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), where subtraction is the same as addition, gives:

\[ x^3\equiv x+1\pmod{x^3+x+1} \]
The folding rule

Whenever \(x^3\) appears, we can replace it with \(x+1\). That is the toy folding rule.

Worked example 1: reduce \(x^3+x^2+1\)

Reduce modulo:

\[ m(x)=x^3+x+1 \]

We use the rule:

\[ x^3\equiv x+1 \]

Replace \(x^3\):

\[ x^3+x^2+1\equiv (x+1)+x^2+1 \]

Combine like terms over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\):

\[ x^2+x+(1+1)=x^2+x \]
Reduced result
\[ x^3+x^2+1\equiv x^2+x\pmod{x^3+x+1} \]

Worked example 2: reduce \(x^4+x^2\)

With the same toy modulus \(m(x)=x^3+x+1\), we need to reduce \(x^4\). Multiply the folding rule by \(x\):

\[ x^3\equiv x+1 \] \[ x^4\equiv x(x+1)=x^2+x \]

Now substitute into the polynomial:

\[ x^4+x^2\equiv (x^2+x)+x^2 \] \[ x^2+x^2+x=x \]
Reduced result
\[ x^4+x^2\equiv x\pmod{x^3+x+1} \]

The AES modulus polynomial

AES uses a degree-8 irreducible polynomial:

\[ m(x)=x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

In hexadecimal, the lower terms \(x^4+x^3+x+1\) correspond to 0x1B. The full polynomial is often associated with 0x11B, because the \(x^8\) term is the ninth bit.

\[ x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad\texttt{0x11B} \]

The AES folding rule for \(x^8\)

Since AES reduces modulo:

\[ m(x)=x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

we treat it as equivalent to 0:

\[ x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\equiv0 \]

Rearranging gives:

\[ x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

The important AES memory hook

In AES, whenever a left shift creates an \(x^8\) term, that high term is folded back as \(x^4+x^3+x+1\), which corresponds to XORing with 0x1B at the byte level.

Worked example 3: reduce \(x^8\) with the AES rule

Use:

\[ x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

Therefore:

\[ x^8\bmod (x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1)=x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

In byte language:

\[ x^4+x^3+x+1=00011011_2=\texttt{0x1B} \]

Worked example 4: reduce \(x^8+x^4+x^2\)

Start with:

\[ x^8+x^4+x^2 \]

Replace \(x^8\):

\[ (x^4+x^3+x+1)+x^4+x^2 \]

Combine terms over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\). The two \(x^4\) terms cancel:

\[ x^3+x^2+x+1 \]
Reduced result
\[ x^8+x^4+x^2\equiv x^3+x^2+x+1 \]

Why irreducible polynomials matter

In integer modular arithmetic, using a prime modulus gives a clean arithmetic world where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. For polynomials, an irreducible polynomial plays a similar role.

Definition: irreducible polynomial

A polynomial is irreducible over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\) if it cannot be factored into lower-degree nonconstant polynomials whose coefficients are also in \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).

AES uses an irreducible degree-8 polynomial so the 256 byte-polynomials form a field. In that field, every nonzero byte has a multiplicative inverse. That inverse is essential for the AES S-box.

Analogy

Prime numbers are the “solid stones” of integer modular arithmetic. Irreducible polynomials are the solid stones of polynomial modular arithmetic. You cannot split them into smaller useful factors inside the arithmetic world you are using.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: reducing high powers without a modulus polynomial

You need a chosen modulus polynomial before reduction has a specific meaning. Different modulus polynomials create different folding rules.

Mistake 2: thinking 0x1B is the full AES modulus

The full AES modulus is 0x11B, representing \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\). The byte 0x1B is the lower part \(x^4+x^3+x+1\), which appears after folding \(x^8\).

Mistake 3: using ordinary subtraction signs as if they differ from addition

Over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), subtraction and addition are the same. Moving terms across the congruence does not change signs in a practical way.

Mistake 4: expecting reduction to mean decimal remainder

This is polynomial remainder, not decimal integer remainder. We reduce powers of \(x\), not ordinary base-10 values.

Practice problems

  1. With toy modulus \(m(x)=x^3+x+1\), reduce \(x^3+x^2+1\).
    Show solution

    Since \(x^3\equiv x+1\):

    \[ x^3+x^2+1\equiv(x+1)+x^2+1=x^2+x \]
  2. With toy modulus \(m(x)=x^3+x+1\), reduce \(x^4+x^2\).
    Show solution

    Since \(x^4\equiv x(x+1)=x^2+x\):

    \[ x^4+x^2\equiv(x^2+x)+x^2=x \]
  3. Using the AES rule, reduce \(x^8\).
    Show solution
    \[ x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1 \]
  4. Using the AES rule, reduce \(x^8+x^4+x^2\).
    Show solution
    \[ x^8+x^4+x^2\equiv(x^4+x^3+x+1)+x^4+x^2 \] \[ =x^3+x^2+x+1 \]
  5. Why does AES need an irreducible polynomial rather than just any polynomial?
    Show solution

    An irreducible polynomial makes the nonzero byte-polynomials behave like field elements, so every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. AES needs those inverses for the S-box.

Why this matters for cryptography

Polynomial reduction is the mechanism that keeps finite-field multiplication closed inside a fixed-size world.

  • Byte-polynomial multiplication can produce powers above \(x^7\).
  • Reduction folds those high powers back into byte-sized form.
  • AES uses \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\), represented as 0x11B.
  • The folded form of \(x^8\) is \(x^4+x^3+x+1\), represented as 0x1B.
  • Using an irreducible polynomial gives a field, which supports nonzero multiplicative inverses.

One-page recap

What to remember
  • Polynomial multiplication can create powers above \(x^7\).
  • Byte-sized polynomial results must have degree at most 7.
  • Polynomial reduction means taking a remainder modulo a polynomial.
  • The modulus polynomial creates the folding rule.
  • AES uses \(m(x)=x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\).
  • The full AES modulus is represented as 0x11B.
  • The folded form of \(x^8\) is \(x^4+x^3+x+1\), represented as 0x1B.
  • Irreducible polynomials are the polynomial-world analogue of primes.
  • Irreducibility matters because it helps create a field where nonzero elements have inverses.
