How to use this workbook

Polynomial reduction is a folding process. Use the modulus polynomial to replace high powers of \(x\) with lower-degree expressions. Then combine like terms over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), remembering that duplicate terms cancel.

Quick reference

Toy modulus rule

For the toy modulus:

\[ m(x)=x^3+x+1 \] \[ x^3\equiv x+1 \] \[ x^4\equiv x^2+x \]
AES modulus rule

For AES:

\[ m(x)=x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1 \] \[ \texttt{0x11B}\longleftrightarrow x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1 \] \[ x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1 \] \[ x^4+x^3+x+1\longleftrightarrow\texttt{0x1B} \]

Note for self-study

Reduction is always tied to a modulus polynomial. Once the folding rule is chosen, substitute high powers and cancel duplicates over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

A1. Polynomial reduction means folding high powers of \(x\) back into a lower-degree polynomial using a chosen modulus polynomial.

A2. A modulus polynomial is the polynomial used as the reduction rule, similar to how an integer modulus controls wraparound in modular arithmetic.

A3. An irreducible polynomial cannot be factored into lower-degree nonconstant polynomials over the coefficient field being used.

A4. Polynomial multiplication can create powers higher than \(x^7\), so reduction is needed to bring the result back into byte-sized form.

Part H: Irreducible polynomial concepts

H1 sample answer. Prime numbers cannot be factored into smaller positive integer factors other than 1 and themselves. Irreducible polynomials similarly cannot be factored into lower-degree nonconstant polynomials in the field being used.

H2 sample answer. AES uses an irreducible polynomial so the byte-polynomials form a field. In a field, every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse.

H3 sample answer. The AES S-box uses multiplicative inverses in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\). Without reliable inverses for nonzero bytes, that construction would not work properly.
