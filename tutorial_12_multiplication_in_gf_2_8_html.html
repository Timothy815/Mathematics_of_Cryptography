Multiplication in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\)

AES-style byte multiplication: multiply byte-polynomials over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), then reduce by \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\).

← Module 11 🏠 Dashboard Module 13 →

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

We have now built all the pieces: bytes as polynomials, addition as XOR, polynomial multiplication, and reduction by an irreducible polynomial. This tutorial combines them into multiplication in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\), the byte-sized finite field used by AES.

The recipe is simple to say but rich to understand: multiply the two byte-polynomials, combine like terms using XOR, then reduce high powers using the AES modulus. The byte is the room; the modulus is the rule that keeps every product inside the room.

Learning objectives

After completing this module you will be able to
  • Explain the three-stage recipe for GF(2⁸) multiplication: convert, multiply, reduce.
  • Compute xtime(a) for any byte using both the polynomial view and the byte-level shortcut.
  • Multiply any two bytes in GF(2⁸) by building an xtime chain and XORing selected pieces.
  • Verify a GF(2⁸) product using full polynomial multiplication and reduction.
  • Explain why 0x1B appears in xtime and why 0x11B is the full modulus.

What you should know first

You should be comfortable converting bytes to polynomials, adding polynomials over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), multiplying polynomials over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), and reducing high powers with the folding rule:

\[ x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

The big idea

Intuition first

Multiplication in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\) is ordinary-looking polynomial multiplication followed by special polynomial wraparound. Multiply first. Fold second. XOR whenever terms combine.

Definition: multiplication in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\)

Formal definition

To multiply two bytes in AES's \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\), interpret them as polynomials over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\), multiply those polynomials, and reduce the result modulo:

\[ m(x)=x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

Written compactly:

\[ a\cdot b\quad\text{in }\mathrm{GF}(2^8) \quad=\quad a(x)b(x)\bmod m(x) \]

The AES modulus review

AES uses:

\[ m(x)=x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

So:

\[ x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1 \]

At the byte level, the folded part is:

\[ x^4+x^3+x+1=00011011_2=\texttt{0x1B} \]

Worked example 1: \(\texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x03}\)

Convert both bytes to polynomials:

\[ \texttt{0x57}=01010111_2=x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \] \[ \texttt{0x03}=00000011_2=x+1 \]

Step 1: Multiply the polynomials

\[ (x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)(x+1) \]

Multiply by \(x\):

\[ x^7+x^5+x^3+x^2+x \]

Multiply by \(1\):

\[ x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \]

Add partial products over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\):

\[ x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+1 \]

Step 2: Check whether reduction is needed

The highest power is \(x^7\). That already fits in a byte-polynomial, so no reduction is needed.

Step 3: Convert back to a byte

Power\(x^7\)\(x^6\)\(x^5\)\(x^4\)\(x^3\)\(x^2\)\(x\)\(1\)
Coefficient11111001
\[ x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+1=11111001_2=\texttt{0xF9} \]
Result
\[ \texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x03}=\texttt{0xF9}\quad\text{in }\mathrm{GF}(2^8) \]

The special case: multiplying by \(x\), or 0x02

Multiplying by \(x\) shifts every term up by one power. At the byte level, that looks like a left shift. But if an \(x^8\) term appears, we must fold it back using 0x1B.

Definition: xtime

\(\operatorname{xtime}(a)\) means multiply byte \(a\) by \(x\), which is the same as multiplying by 0x02 in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\).

CasePolynomial viewByte-level rule
No \(x^7\) term before shifting No \(x^8\) term is created. Left shift only.
There is an \(x^7\) term before shifting Shifting creates \(x^8\), which folds to \(x^4+x^3+x+1\). Left shift, then XOR with 0x1B.

Worked example 2: \(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x57})\)

Since:

\[ \texttt{0x57}=x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \]

multiply by \(x\):

\[ x(x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1)=x^7+x^5+x^3+x^2+x \]

No \(x^8\) term appears, so no reduction is needed.

\[ x^7+x^5+x^3+x^2+x=10101110_2=\texttt{0xAE} \]
Result
\[ \operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x57})=\texttt{0xAE} \]

Worked example 3: \(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x83})\)

Convert the byte:

\[ \texttt{0x83}=10000011_2=x^7+x+1 \]

Multiply by \(x\):

\[ x(x^7+x+1)=x^8+x^2+x \]

Now reduce \(x^8\):

\[ x^8+x^2+x\equiv(x^4+x^3+x+1)+x^2+x \]

The two \(x\) terms cancel:

\[ x^4+x^3+x^2+1 \]

Convert back to a byte:

\[ x^4+x^3+x^2+1=00011101_2=\texttt{0x1D} \]
Result
\[ \operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x83})=\texttt{0x1D} \]

The byte-level xtime shortcut

The polynomial process becomes a compact byte rule:

If the top bit is 0:
\[ \operatorname{xtime}(a)=a\ll1 \]
If the top bit is 1:
\[ \operatorname{xtime}(a)=(a\ll1)\oplus\texttt{0x1B} \]
Keep only the low 8 bits after shifting.

Check with 0x83

\[ \texttt{0x83}\ll1=\texttt{0x106} \]

Keep the low byte:

\[ \texttt{0x06} \]

XOR with 0x1B:

\[ \texttt{0x06}\oplus\texttt{0x1B}=\texttt{0x1D} \]

Multiplication by 0x03

Since:

\[ \texttt{0x03}=x+1 \]

multiplying by 0x03 means:

\[ a\cdot\texttt{0x03}=a\cdot(x+1)=a\cdot x+a \]

In byte form:

\[ a\cdot\texttt{0x03}=\operatorname{xtime}(a)\oplus a \]

Example with 0x57

\[ \operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x57})=\texttt{0xAE} \] \[ \texttt{0xAE}\oplus\texttt{0x57}=\texttt{0xF9} \]

This matches the polynomial result from worked example 1.

General multiplication by repeated xtime and XOR

To multiply by a byte with several 1 bits, split that byte into powers of \(x\). Then use repeated \(\operatorname{xtime}\) calls and XOR the needed pieces.

Example: multiply by 0x0B

\[ \texttt{0x0B}=00001011_2=x^3+x+1 \]

So:

\[ a\cdot\texttt{0x0B}=x^3a\oplus xa\oplus a \]

Byte-level version:

\[ a\cdot\texttt{0x0B}=\operatorname{xtime}(\operatorname{xtime}(\operatorname{xtime}(a)))\oplus\operatorname{xtime}(a)\oplus a \]

Worked example 4: \(\texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x13}\)

This is a classic AES finite-field multiplication example.

\[ \texttt{0x13}=00010011_2=x^4+x+1 \]

So:

\[ \texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x13}=x^4a\oplus xa\oplus a \] where \(a=\texttt{0x57}\).
PowerMeaningValue
\(a\)Original byte0x57
\(xa\)\(\operatorname{xtime}(a)\)0xAE
\(x^2a\)\(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0xAE})\)0x47
\(x^3a\)\(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x47})\)0x8E
\(x^4a\)\(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x8E})\)0x07

Now XOR the pieces required by \(x^4+x+1\):

\[ \texttt{0x07}\oplus\texttt{0xAE}\oplus\texttt{0x57}=\texttt{0xFE} \]
Result
\[ \texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x13}=\texttt{0xFE}\quad\text{in }\mathrm{GF}(2^8) \]

Polynomial check for \(\texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x13}\)

In polynomial form:

\[ \texttt{0x57}=x^6+x^4+x^2+x+1 \] \[ \texttt{0x13}=x^4+x+1 \]

The unreduced product is:

\[ x^{10}+x^8+x^7+x^5+x^4+x^3+1 \]

Reducing modulo \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\) gives:

\[ x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+x^2+x \]

Convert to binary:

\[ 11111110_2=\texttt{0xFE} \]

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: using integer multiplication

\(\texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x13}\) in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\) is not ordinary integer multiplication. It is polynomial multiplication plus reduction.

Mistake 2: forgetting that addition is XOR

Partial products are combined with XOR, not decimal addition and not binary addition with carries.

Mistake 3: confusing 0x11B and 0x1B

0x11B is the full AES modulus. 0x1B is the lower folded part used when an \(x^8\) term appears.

Mistake 4: forgetting to keep only 8 bits after a left shift

When using the byte-level shortcut, a shift may produce a ninth bit. Keep the low byte and XOR with 0x1B if the original top bit was 1.

Practice problems

  1. Compute \(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x57})\).
    Show solution
    \[ \texttt{0x57}=01010111_2 \] \[ \text{left shift}=10101110_2=\texttt{0xAE} \]

    The top bit was 0, so no XOR with 0x1B is needed.

  2. Compute \(\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x83})\).
    Show solution
    \[ \texttt{0x83}\ll1=\texttt{0x106} \] \[ \text{low byte}=\texttt{0x06} \] \[ \texttt{0x06}\oplus\texttt{0x1B}=\texttt{0x1D} \]
  3. Compute \(\texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x03}\).
    Show solution
    \[ \texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x03}=\operatorname{xtime}(\texttt{0x57})\oplus\texttt{0x57} \] \[ =\texttt{0xAE}\oplus\texttt{0x57}=\texttt{0xF9} \]
  4. Compute \(\texttt{0x57}\cdot\texttt{0x13}\).
    Show solution
    \[ \texttt{0x13}=x^4+x+1 \] \[ x^4a=\texttt{0x07},\quad xa=\texttt{0xAE},\quad a=\texttt{0x57} \] \[ \texttt{0x07}\oplus\texttt{0xAE}\oplus\texttt{0x57}=\texttt{0xFE} \]
  5. Why does multiplying by 0x02 sometimes require XORing with 0x1B?
    Show solution

    Multiplying by 0x02 means multiplying by \(x\). If the original byte has an \(x^7\) term, shifting creates \(x^8\). AES reduces \(x^8\) to \(x^4+x^3+x+1\), which is 0x1B.

Why this matters for cryptography

Multiplication in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\) is not a decorative side alley. It is used directly inside AES.

  • AES MixColumns uses multiplication by fixed field elements such as 0x02 and 0x03.
  • The AES S-box uses multiplicative inverses in \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\).
  • The irreducible modulus keeps byte multiplication closed inside 256 possible byte values.
  • XOR addition keeps the arithmetic efficient in hardware and software.
  • Understanding this removes much of the mystery from AES's algebraic core.

One-page recap

What to remember
  • \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\) is a byte-sized finite field with 256 elements.
  • Each byte is treated as a polynomial with coefficients 0 or 1.
  • Addition is XOR.
  • Multiplication starts as polynomial multiplication over \(\mathrm{GF}(2)\).
  • The product is reduced modulo \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\).
  • The full AES modulus is 0x11B.
  • The folded lower part is 0x1B.
  • Multiplying by 0x02 is \(\operatorname{xtime}\).
  • If \(\operatorname{xtime}\) shifts out a top bit, XOR with 0x1B.
  • Multiplying by 0x03 means \(\operatorname{xtime}(a)\oplus a\).
