How to use this workbook

Focus on the building blocks. A word is four bytes. A round key is four words. RotWord moves bytes inside a word. SubWord substitutes each byte through the AES S-box. The full Rcon calculation comes next, but this workbook prepares the machinery.

Quick reference

AES-128 key expansion counts
  • Original key: 16 bytes
  • One word: 4 bytes
  • One round key: 4 words = 16 bytes
  • Total round keys: 11
  • Total words: 44
  • Total round-key material: 176 bytes
Core transformations
\[ \operatorname{RotWord}([a_0,a_1,a_2,a_3])=[a_1,a_2,a_3,a_0] \] \[ \operatorname{SubWord}([a,b,c,d])=[S(a),S(b),S(c),S(d)] \]

Part H: XOR-chain reasoning

WordFormula
\(w_5\)\(w_5=w_1\oplus w_4\)

H1 sample answer. The first word of each new round key receives special treatment so the key schedule introduces rotation, S-box nonlinearity, and round constants before the rest of the words chain forward by XOR.

Part I: Concept checks

I1 sample answer. AES-128 needs 11 round keys, and each round key is 16 bytes. Therefore it needs \(11\times16=176\) bytes of round-key material.

I2 sample answer. SubWord introduces nonlinearity into the key schedule by applying the AES S-box to each byte of a word.

I3 sample answer. The cipher key is the original secret key supplied to AES. Round keys are 16-byte subkeys derived from that cipher key and used at different stages of encryption and decryption.
