Nonces, IVs, Misuse Resistance, and Why GCM Fails Hard When Reused

Why AES-GCM depends on nonce uniqueness, how nonce reuse damages confidentiality and authentication, and what misuse resistance means.

🏠 Dashboard

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

AES-GCM is fast and widely used, but it has a sharp requirement: never reuse the same nonce with the same key. This tutorial explains what nonces and IVs are, why uniqueness matters, what goes wrong when GCM is reused, and what misuse-resistant designs try to improve.

A nonce is not a secret jewel. It is a serial number. But in GCM, using the same serial number twice with the same key can turn a strong cryptographic machine into a leaky contraption with smoke coming out of the gears.

What you should know first

You should know that AES-GCM uses CTR-style encryption for confidentiality and GHASH for authentication. You should also know that CTR mode creates keystream blocks from a nonce/counter structure, and that GHASH produces a tag over AAD and ciphertext.

Nonce versus IV

The terms nonce and IV are sometimes used interchangeably, but they emphasize different ideas.

TermCore ideaMust be secret?Main requirement
NonceNumber used once.No.Unique under the same key.
IVInitialization Vector.Usually no.Depends on the mode: unique, random, unpredictable, or some combination.
Important wording

For AES-GCM, the practical rule is: the nonce/IV does not need to be secret, but it must not repeat with the same key.

Uniqueness is not the same as randomness

A nonce can be random, but randomness is not the main goal. The main goal is uniqueness under the key. A counter can be perfectly predictable and still safe for GCM if it never repeats with the same key.

Nonce strategyPredictable?Can be safe for GCM?Main risk
Simple counterYesYes, if never repeated with the same key.Counter reset, rollback, or reuse after restart.
Random 96-bit nonceNoOften used, but collision risk must be managed.Birthday collisions at high scale.
Timestamp onlyOften yesDangerous by itself.Multiple encryptions in same timestamp unit.
Fixed nonceYesNo.Immediate reuse under same key.
Friendly rule

GCM does not care whether the nonce is surprising. It cares whether the nonce is repeated. Surprise is optional; uniqueness is mandatory.

Why GCM reuse breaks confidentiality

The encryption side of GCM is CTR-like. CTR-style encryption creates a keystream and XORs it with plaintext:

\[ C=P\oplus S \]

If the same key and nonce are reused, the same keystream can be used again:

\[ C_1=P_1\oplus S \] \[ C_2=P_2\oplus S \]

Now XOR the two ciphertexts:

\[ C_1\oplus C_2=(P_1\oplus S)\oplus(P_2\oplus S) \] \[ C_1\oplus C_2=P_1\oplus P_2 \]

The keystream cancels. The attacker may not immediately know both plaintexts, but they now have a dangerous relationship between them. If one plaintext is known or guessed, the other may be exposed.

Confidentiality failure

Reusing the same GCM nonce with the same key can repeat the CTR keystream. Repeated keystream is one of the classic disasters of stream-style encryption.

Why GCM reuse also harms authentication

GCM nonce reuse is worse than ordinary CTR nonce reuse because GCM also uses GHASH authentication. Reuse can give attackers algebraic information about the authentication process and may make tag forgery possible under certain conditions.

High-level explanation

GHASH is algebraic. When the same key and nonce are reused across messages, the attacker can compare relationships between ciphertexts and tags. Those relationships may leak information about the GHASH subkey or allow valid-looking tags to be created for modified data.

The exact forgery math is more advanced, but the practical rule is simple: in AES-GCM, nonce reuse can damage both confidentiality and integrity.

Why 96-bit nonces are common in GCM

AES-GCM commonly uses 96-bit nonces. This size is efficient because GCM can build the initial counter block directly in the standard case. Other nonce lengths can be processed, but they require extra GHASH processing to derive the starting counter block.

Nonce lengthGCM handlingPractical note
96 bitsEfficient standard path.Commonly recommended and widely used.
Other lengthsGHASH-derived starting counter.Valid in the standard, but less direct.

Safer nonce-management practices

Good nonce management is engineering, not decoration. The system must prevent repeats even during restarts, crashes, distributed writes, and key rotation.

PracticePurpose
Use a well-designed library API.Reduces the chance of manual nonce mistakes.
Use counters with persistent state when appropriate.Prevents reuse after restart.
Partition nonce ranges for multiple writers.Prevents distributed collisions.
Rotate keys before nonce space is exhausted.Prevents counter wraparound under one key.
Never use fixed nonces.Prevents immediate catastrophic reuse.

What misuse resistance means

Definition: misuse resistance

Misuse resistance means a cryptographic design remains safer under certain common mistakes than a more fragile design would. It does not mean mistakes are harmless. It means the failure is less catastrophic or more limited.

Some authenticated encryption modes are designed to tolerate nonce reuse better than AES-GCM. One important example family is AES-GCM-SIV.

ModeNonce reuse behaviorPractical idea
AES-GCMFails hard under nonce reuse.Fast and common, but requires strict uniqueness.
AES-GCM-SIVDesigned to be more nonce-misuse resistant.Computes a synthetic IV from message/authentication data.
Other SIV-style modesGenerally more robust against nonce reuse.Often trade some performance or usage properties for safety.
Plain-language version

Misuse-resistant modes are like tools with guards around the blade. You still should not put your hand there, but the design tries harder not to punish one mistake with total disaster.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: thinking nonces must be secret

In GCM, nonces do not need to be secret. They need to be unique under the key.

Mistake 2: confusing random with guaranteed unique

Random values can collide. Whether the risk is acceptable depends on size, volume, and system design.

Mistake 3: using timestamps alone

Timestamps may repeat when multiple messages are encrypted in the same time unit or when clocks move backward.

Mistake 4: assuming misuse resistance means mistake-proof

Misuse resistance reduces damage for some mistakes. It does not make careless cryptography safe.

Practice problems

  1. Does an AES-GCM nonce need to be secret?
    Show solution

    No. It must be unique under the same key, but it does not need to be secret.

  2. What happens if the same GCM key and nonce produce the same keystream for two messages?
    Show solution

    XORing the ciphertexts cancels the keystream and reveals \(P_1\oplus P_2\), a dangerous relationship between plaintexts.

  3. Why can nonce reuse harm GCM authentication too?
    Show solution

    GHASH is algebraic, and reused key/nonce pairs can reveal relationships between ciphertexts and tags that may support forgery.

  4. Why are 96-bit nonces common in GCM?
    Show solution

    They use GCM's efficient standard path for constructing the initial counter block.

  5. What does misuse resistance mean?
    Show solution

    It means a design remains safer or fails less catastrophically under certain common mistakes, though mistakes are still not harmless.

Why this matters for cryptography

Nonce management looks like bookkeeping, but in AES-GCM it is part of the security proof's foundation. A perfect AES implementation and a perfect GHASH implementation can still fail if the nonce system repeats values. In cryptography, the envelope, stamp, and serial number are part of the machine.

  • GCM nonces do not need secrecy.
  • GCM nonces must be unique under the same key.
  • Nonce reuse repeats CTR-style keystream.
  • Nonce reuse can also damage authentication.
  • Counters, random values, and distributed systems all require careful design.
  • Misuse-resistant modes try to reduce damage from certain mistakes.
