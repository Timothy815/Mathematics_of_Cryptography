How to use this workbook

This workbook focuses on the difference between hitting one specific target and finding any matching pair. Most mistakes in this topic come from forgetting that collision search compares many samples against each other.

Note for self-study

The central idea is that pair hunting is easier than target hunting. Collision search benefits from many pairwise comparisons.

Part B: Birthday paradox basics

B1 sample answer. The birthday paradox says that in a surprisingly small group, there is a good chance that at least two people share a birthday because every person can be compared with every other person.

QuestionWhat it asksWhy it differs
Does someone share my birthday?Everyone is compared to one fixed target.Only one birthday counts.
Do any two people share a birthday?Every person is compared to every other person.Any matching pair counts.

B2 sample answer. Any-pair matching gives many more chances for success. You are not trying to match one specific birthday; you are checking all pairs in the room.

Part E: The square-root rule

ExpressionMeaning or simplified form
\(N\)The number of possible outputs.
\(\sqrt{N}\)The rough sample scale where collisions become likely.
\(N=2^n\)An \(n\)-bit hash has \(2^n\) possible digests.
\(\sqrt{2^n}\)The square root of the output space.
\(2^{n/2}\)The rough collision-search scale for an ideal \(n\)-bit hash.

E1 sample answer. An \(n\)-bit hash has \(2^n\) possible outputs. Collision search becomes likely around the square root of that output space, and \(\sqrt{2^n}=2^{n/2}\).

G1 sample answer. A 256-bit digest gives a huge preimage space of about \(2^{256}\) and roughly \(2^{128}\) collision work under ideal assumptions, which is a strong practical security margin.

Part M: Concept checks

M1 sample answer. The birthday paradox says that any matching pair appears sooner than intuition expects because many pairwise comparisons are happening. For hashes, messages are like people and digests are like birthdays, so collision search benefits from the same pair-counting effect.

M2 sample answer. An \(n\)-bit hash has \(2^n\) possible outputs. Collision search becomes likely around the square root of that space, and \(\sqrt{2^n}=2^{n/2}\).

M3 sample answer. Preimage search is target hunting. The attacker must hit one specific digest, so pair-counting does not help in the same broad way.

M4 sample answer. The birthday paradox explains why hash collision attacks are easier than preimage attacks. Collision attacks look for any matching pair, so the number of pairwise comparisons grows quickly as more messages are hashed. If a hash has \(N\) possible outputs, collisions become likely around \(\sqrt{N}\). For an \(n\)-bit hash, that becomes \(2^{n/2}\), which is why a 256-bit hash gives roughly 128-bit collision security.
