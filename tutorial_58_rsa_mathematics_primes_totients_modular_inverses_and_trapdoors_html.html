RSA Mathematics: Primes, Totients, Modular Inverses, and Trapdoors

How RSA builds a public/private key pair from two primes, hides the factorization of \(n\), uses Euler’s totient, and creates inverse exponents for encryption, decryption, signing, and verification.

🏠 Dashboard

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

Tutorial 57 studied Diffie-Hellman, where the hard problem is related to discrete logarithms. RSA uses a different trapdoor idea. Multiplying two large primes is easy, but factoring their product is hard. If you know the primes, you can compute secret information that lets you build the private key.

RSA is a locked clockwork box. The public can see the box, turn one crank, and create a scrambled result. The owner knows how the gears were built, because the owner knows the two hidden primes. That knowledge is the trapdoor.

What you should know first

You should know modular arithmetic, modular exponentiation, and modular inverses. You should also know the public-key idea: public information may be shared, while private information gives the owner a special capability.

Step 1: choose primes and compute \(n\)

RSA starts by choosing two large primes \(p\) and \(q\). Their product becomes the public modulus:

\[ n=pq \]

The security trapdoor begins here. Multiplying primes is easy. Factoring \(n\) back into \(p\) and \(q\) is hard when the primes are very large and chosen correctly.

Memory hook

Multiplication is mixing paint. Factoring is trying to recover the exact original paint tubes from the final color.

Step 2: compute Euler’s totient

Euler’s totient \(\varphi(n)\) counts how many positive integers less than \(n\) are relatively prime to \(n\). For RSA, when \(n=pq\) and \(p,q\) are prime:

\[ \varphi(n)=(p-1)(q-1) \]

Knowing \(p\) and \(q\) makes \(\varphi(n)\) easy to compute. Not knowing \(p\) and \(q\) makes it hard.

Step 3: choose the public exponent \(e\)

The public exponent \(e\) must be relatively prime to \(\varphi(n)\). In other words:

\[ \gcd(e,\varphi(n))=1 \]

This condition ensures that \(e\) has a modular inverse modulo \(\varphi(n)\). That inverse becomes the private exponent \(d\).

Step 4: compute the private exponent \(d\)

The private exponent \(d\) is the modular inverse of \(e\) modulo \(\varphi(n)\):

\[ d\equiv e^{-1}\pmod{\varphi(n)} \] \[ ed\equiv1\pmod{\varphi(n)} \]

This inverse relationship is the gear mesh that makes RSA work. Raising to \(e\) and then to \(d\) returns the message under the right conditions.

Worked toy RSA key generation

Use tiny primes so the arithmetic is visible. This is not secure.

\[ p=5,\quad q=11 \] \[ n=pq=5\cdot11=55 \] \[ \varphi(n)=(5-1)(11-1)=4\cdot10=40 \]

Choose \(e=3\). Since \(\gcd(3,40)=1\), it is allowed. Now find \(d\) such that:

\[ 3d\equiv1\pmod{40} \]

\(d=27\), because:

\[ 3\cdot27=81 \] \[ 81\equiv1\pmod{40} \]

RSA encryption and decryption formulas

In textbook RSA form, encryption raises the message number \(m\) to the public exponent \(e\):

\[ c\equiv m^e\pmod n \]

Decryption raises the ciphertext number \(c\) to the private exponent \(d\):

\[ m\equiv c^d\pmod n \]
Important security warning

Textbook RSA is not secure for real encryption by itself. Real RSA encryption must use secure padding such as OAEP. The formulas here are for understanding the core mathematics.

Worked toy RSA encryption example

Use the toy public key \((n,e)=(55,3)\). Let the message be:

\[ m=7 \]

Encrypt:

\[ c\equiv 7^3\pmod{55} \] \[ 7^3=343 \] \[ 343\equiv13\pmod{55} \]

So the ciphertext is:

\[ c=13 \]

Worked toy RSA decryption example

Decrypt with \(d=27\):

\[ m\equiv13^{27}\pmod{55} \]

The exponent is large, but modular exponentiation keeps reducing along the way. The result is:

\[ 13^{27}\equiv7\pmod{55} \]

The original message \(m=7\) returns.

Why RSA works

RSA works because \(e\) and \(d\) are inverses modulo \(\varphi(n)\). That means there is some integer \(k\) such that:

\[ ed=1+k\varphi(n) \]

So decrypting after encrypting gives:

\[ c^d\equiv(m^e)^d\equiv m^{ed}\pmod n \] \[ m^{ed}=m^{1+k\varphi(n)} \]

Euler’s theorem is the deep support beam under this structure. The simplified intuition is: the exponents are chosen so that the private exponent undoes the public exponent in the modular system.

The RSA trapdoor

The public knows \(n\) and \(e\). To find \(d\), one needs \(\varphi(n)\). To compute \(\varphi(n)\), one needs \(p\) and \(q\). So the secret factorization is the trapdoor.

RSA signatures preview

RSA can also be used for signatures, but real signatures require secure padding such as PSS. The mathematical role flips: the private exponent creates the signature-like value, and the public exponent verifies it.

\[ s\equiv m^d\pmod n \] \[ m\equiv s^e\pmod n \]
Do not overgeneralize

“Encrypt with private key” is a common but sloppy phrase. For signatures, think: private key signs, public key verifies. Real signature schemes include hashing, encoding, and padding rules.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: thinking \(n\) must be secret

The modulus \(n\) is public. The factorization \(p,q\) must stay secret.

Mistake 2: forgetting \(e\) needs an inverse

\(e\) must be relatively prime to \(\varphi(n)\) so that \(d\) exists.

Mistake 3: using textbook RSA in real systems

Real RSA needs padding schemes such as OAEP or PSS.

Mistake 4: saying signatures are simply “private-key encryption”

Signatures have their own rules, padding, hashing, and security goals.

Practice problems

  1. What is \(n\) in RSA?
    Show solution

    \(n=pq\), the product of two primes.

  2. If \(p=7\) and \(q=13\), compute \(n\) and \(\varphi(n)\).
    Show solution

    \(n=7\cdot13=91\). \(\varphi(n)=(7-1)(13-1)=6\cdot12=72\).

  3. What condition must \(e\) satisfy?
    Show solution

    \(\gcd(e,\varphi(n))=1\).

  4. What does \(d\equiv e^{-1}\pmod{\varphi(n)}\) mean?
    Show solution

    It means \(ed\equiv1\pmod{\varphi(n)}\).

  5. What is the RSA trapdoor?
    Show solution

    The hidden factorization \(p,q\), which allows computation of \(\varphi(n)\) and then \(d\).

  6. Why is textbook RSA not enough for real encryption?
    Show solution

    It lacks secure padding and is deterministic/vulnerable. Real RSA encryption uses padding such as OAEP.

Why this matters for cryptography

RSA is one of the classic examples of a trapdoor function. The public can know \(n\) and \(e\), but only someone who knows the hidden factorization can compute the private exponent. The mathematics ties together primes, totients, greatest common divisors, modular inverses, and modular exponentiation.

  • RSA begins with secret primes \(p\) and \(q\).
  • The public modulus is \(n=pq\).
  • For \(n=pq\), \(\varphi(n)=(p-1)(q-1)\).
  • The public exponent \(e\) must satisfy \(\gcd(e,\varphi(n))=1\).
  • The private exponent satisfies \(ed\equiv1\pmod{\varphi(n)}\).
  • Encryption shape: \(c\equiv m^e\pmod n\).
  • Decryption shape: \(m\equiv c^d\pmod n\).
  • The trapdoor is knowledge of \(p,q\).
  • Real RSA requires secure padding and careful implementation.
