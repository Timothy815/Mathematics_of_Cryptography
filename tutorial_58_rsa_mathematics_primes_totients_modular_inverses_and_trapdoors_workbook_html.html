How to use this workbook

Follow the RSA gear train carefully: choose primes, multiply them into \(n\), compute \(\varphi(n)\), choose \(e\), find \(d\), then use modular exponentiation. The arithmetic is toy-sized, but the structure is the real machine.

Quick reference

RSA key construction
\[ n=pq \] \[ \varphi(n)=(p-1)(q-1) \] \[ \gcd(e,\varphi(n))=1 \] \[ d\equiv e^{-1}\pmod{\varphi(n)} \]
RSA operation shapes
\[ c\equiv m^e\pmod n \] \[ m\equiv c^d\pmod n \] \[ ed\equiv1\pmod{\varphi(n)} \]

Part I: Why RSA works

StepExpressionMeaning
1\(ed=1+k\varphi(n)\)\(e\) and \(d\) are inverses modulo \(\varphi(n)\).
2\(c^d\equiv(m^e)^d\pmod n\)Decrypting raises the encrypted value to \(d\).
3\(m^{ed}=m^{1+k\varphi(n)}\)The exponent has the form needed for Euler’s theorem structure.
4Original \(m\) returnsThe private exponent undoes the public exponent under RSA conditions.

I1 sample answer. \(d\) is chosen so that multiplying by \(e\) is equivalent to 1 in the modular exponent cycle. That makes exponentiating by \(d\) undo the effect of exponentiating by \(e\).

J1 sample answer. Knowing \(p,q\) lets the owner compute \(\varphi(n)\), which lets them compute \(d\). Without the factorization, attackers should not be able to find the private exponent.

Part K: RSA signatures preview

K1 sample answer. Signatures are not merely encryption in reverse. Real signature schemes use hashing, encoding, padding, and verification rules. It is clearer to say the private key signs and the public key verifies.

Part O: Concept checks

O1 sample answer. RSA key generation starts by choosing two secret primes \(p\) and \(q\), multiplying them to get \(n=pq\), and computing \(\varphi(n)=(p-1)(q-1)\). Then a public exponent \(e\) is chosen relatively prime to \(\varphi(n)\), and the private exponent \(d\) is computed as the modular inverse of \(e\).

O2 sample answer. Modular inverses matter because \(d\) must undo \(e\) in the RSA exponent structure. The relationship \(ed\equiv1\pmod{\varphi(n)}\) is what allows decryption to recover the original message.

O3 sample answer. The RSA trapdoor is knowledge of the hidden prime factors \(p\) and \(q\). Those factors reveal \(\varphi(n)\), which allows the owner to compute \(d\), the private exponent.

O4 sample answer. This tutorial explained RSA as a public-key trapdoor system based on primes, totients, modular inverses, and modular exponentiation. The public key is \((n,e)\), while the private exponent \(d\) is computed using \(\varphi(n)\), which requires knowing the secret factorization. Textbook RSA shows the arithmetic, but real RSA requires secure padding such as OAEP for encryption and PSS for signatures.
