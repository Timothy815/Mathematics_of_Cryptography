Part N: Concept checks

N1 sample answer. In toy RSA encryption, the sender computes \(c\equiv m^e\pmod n\) using the public key. The owner decrypts by computing \(m\equiv c^d\pmod n\) using the private exponent. In the toy key, \(m=7\) becomes \(c=13\), and decrypting \(13\) returns \(7\).

N2 sample answer. RSA signature intuition reverses the roles: the private exponent creates a signature-like value, and the public exponent verifies it. Real signatures are not raw RSA on the message; they use hashing, encoding, and padding rules.

N3 sample answer. Padding matters because textbook RSA is deterministic, lacks randomness, and has exploitable mathematical structure. Real RSA encryption uses OAEP, and RSA signatures commonly use PSS.

N4 sample answer. This tutorial practiced toy RSA encryption, decryption, and signature intuition. It showed how public exponentiation can be undone by private exponentiation, how repeated squaring helps compute large powers modulo \(n\), and why real RSA must not use the raw textbook formulas directly. Real systems use padding and often hybrid encryption.
