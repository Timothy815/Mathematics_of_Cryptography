Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman and Digital Signatures

Turning elliptic-curve point multiplication into cryptographic work: ECDH shared secrets, KDFs, digital signatures, verification, nonce danger, and authentication.

🏠 Dashboard

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

Tutorial 60 introduced elliptic-curve points, point addition, point doubling, scalar multiplication, and public keys of the form \(Q=dG\). This tutorial uses those ideas for two major cryptographic jobs: key exchange and digital signatures.

ECC gives us two kinds of cryptographic choreography. In ECDH, two dancers arrive at the same hidden point without announcing their steps. In elliptic-curve signatures, one dancer proves they knew the secret rhythm without giving the rhythm away.

What you should know first

You should remember that \(G\) is a public base point, \(d\) is a private scalar, and \(Q=dG\) is a public key point. Scalar multiplication is efficient forward, while recovering \(d\) from \(G\) and \(Q\) is the elliptic-curve discrete logarithm problem.

Why the shared points match

Alice receives Bob’s point \(B=bG\) and multiplies it by her scalar \(a\). Bob receives Alice’s point \(A=aG\) and multiplies it by his scalar \(b\).

\[ S_A=aB=a(bG)=abG \] \[ S_B=bA=b(aG)=baG \] \[ abG=baG \]

The private scalars are applied in different orders, but the final scalar product is the same. The two paths meet at the same shared point.

From shared point to usable keys

The ECDH shared point is not usually used directly as an encryption key. Instead, a representation of the shared point, often its \(x\)-coordinate or an encoded point value depending on the protocol, is fed into a KDF with context.

\[ shared\ material = encode(S) \] \[ traffic\ keys=KDF(shared\ material, transcript, identities, algorithms) \]
KDF inputWhy include it?
Shared materialCore secret produced by ECDH.
TranscriptBinds keys to the exchanged messages.
IdentitiesHelps bind keys to the intended parties.
AlgorithmsPrevents confusion about negotiated cryptographic choices.

ECDH still needs authentication

ECDH protects against passive eavesdroppers when implemented correctly, but unauthenticated ECDH can still be attacked by an active man-in-the-middle.

SituationProblemNeeded defense
Alice accepts any public point as Bob’s.Attacker can substitute their own point.Authenticate Bob’s public key or transcript.
Bob accepts any public point as Alice’s.Attacker can create separate secrets with both sides.Use signatures, certificates, fingerprints, or pre-shared trust.
Protocol omits transcript binding.Keys may not be bound to the exact exchange.Use a KDF with transcript/context.
Core warning

ECDH creates a shared secret. Authentication tells you who shared it with you.

Static and ephemeral ECDH

ECDH can use long-term key pairs, fresh one-time key pairs, or a mixture of both.

TypeMeaningSecurity intuition
Static ECDHLong-term ECDH key pair is reused.Can authenticate identity but gives weaker forward secrecy.
Ephemeral ECDHFresh ECDH key pair is generated for a session.Supports forward secrecy when combined with authentication.
Authenticated ephemeral ECDHFresh ECDH shares are authenticated.Common modern secure-channel pattern.
Definition: forward secrecy

Forward secrecy means that compromise of a long-term key later does not automatically reveal past session keys.

ECDSA shape, without drowning in formulas

The Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm uses a private key, a message hash, and a fresh per-signature nonce. It outputs a pair of numbers, often written:

\[ signature=(r,s) \]

At a high level:

StepIdea
1Hash the message.
2Use private key \(d\) and a fresh nonce \(k\).
3Compute signature values \((r,s)\).
4Verifier uses public key \(Q\), message hash, and \((r,s)\) to check validity.
Why no full ECDSA formula yet?

The full ECDSA equations are important, but this tutorial’s goal is conceptual alignment. We first need the roles clear: private key signs, public key verifies, and nonce handling is critical.

The signing nonce danger

Some elliptic-curve signature schemes, especially ECDSA, require a fresh secret nonce for each signature. Reusing or leaking that nonce can reveal the private key.

Nonce problemConsequence
Same nonce reused for two messagesPrivate key may be recoverable.
Nonce partially predictablePrivate key may become vulnerable.
Weak random generatorSignatures may leak secrets.
Deterministic nonce generated incorrectlyPrivate key may be exposed.
The bright red lever

In ECDSA, nonce failure can become private-key failure. This is one of the most important practical lessons in elliptic-curve signatures.

EdDSA preview

EdDSA is a modern elliptic-curve signature family designed to avoid several historical foot-guns. It commonly uses deterministic nonce generation derived from the private key material and the message, rather than relying directly on fresh random nonce generation for every signature.

Signature familyNonce handling intuitionCommon examples
ECDSANeeds a unique secret nonce per signature; failures are dangerous.ECDSA over P-256 and similar curves.
EdDSAOften deterministic nonce generation, reducing random-number failure risk.Ed25519, Ed448.
Still use libraries

EdDSA is friendlier in several ways, but signature implementation remains delicate. Use vetted libraries and protocols.

ECDH versus elliptic-curve signatures

JobUses private scalar to...Public key used to...Main result
ECDHCombine with peer public point.Let peer compute matching shared point.Shared secret material.
SignatureProduce proof for a message.Verify the proof.Authenticity and integrity.
Memory hook

ECDH says, “Let us build a shared secret.” Signatures say, “I can prove this came from the private key holder.”

How protocols combine them

Modern secure channels often combine ephemeral ECDH with digital signatures or certificates.

1. Alice and Bob exchange ephemeral ECDH public points.
2. They authenticate the exchange using signatures, certificates, or existing trust.
3. Both compute the ECDH shared point.
4. A KDF derives traffic keys from the shared material and transcript.
5. Symmetric AEAD protects the data.
ComponentJob
Ephemeral ECDHCreates fresh shared secret material.
Signature/certificateAuthenticates who is participating.
KDFTurns shared material into context-bound keys.
AEADEncrypts and authenticates application data.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: thinking ECDH alone proves identity

ECDH creates a shared secret. Authentication is separate.

Mistake 2: using raw shared point directly as an encryption key

Feed encoded shared material into a KDF with transcript and context.

Mistake 3: treating signature nonces casually

In ECDSA, repeated or predictable nonces can expose the private key.

Mistake 4: mixing key purposes

Key-exchange keys and signing keys should be used according to protocol rules and key-usage expectations.

Practice problems

  1. In ECDH, Alice has private scalar \(a\). What is Alice’s public point?
    Show solution

    \(A=aG\).

  2. In ECDH, Bob has private scalar \(b\). What is Bob’s public point?
    Show solution

    \(B=bG\).

  3. Why do Alice and Bob compute the same shared point?
    Show solution

    Alice computes \(aB=a(bG)=abG\), and Bob computes \(bA=b(aG)=abG\).

  4. What should happen to the ECDH shared point before it becomes encryption keys?
    Show solution

    It should be encoded and fed into a KDF with transcript and context.

  5. What does a digital signature prove, at a high level?
    Show solution

    That someone with the private key produced a valid signature for the message, assuming the scheme and key are trusted.

  6. Why are ECDSA nonces dangerous if reused?
    Show solution

    Nonce reuse can reveal enough algebraic information to recover the private key.

Why this matters for cryptography

ECDH and elliptic-curve signatures are among the most important modern uses of public-key cryptography. ECDH creates shared secret material efficiently. Signatures authenticate messages and protocol transcripts. Real systems combine them with KDFs, certificates, transcript binding, and symmetric authenticated encryption.

  • ECDH public points have the form \(A=aG\) and \(B=bG\).
  • Alice computes \(aB=abG\).
  • Bob computes \(bA=abG\).
  • The raw shared point must feed a KDF.
  • ECDH alone does not prove identity.
  • Digital signatures use private keys to sign and public keys to verify.
  • ECDSA signatures are often written as \((r,s)\).
  • ECDSA nonce reuse can expose the private key.
  • EdDSA reduces some nonce-generation risks through deterministic design choices.
  • Modern secure channels often use ephemeral ECDH plus signatures/certificates plus AEAD.
