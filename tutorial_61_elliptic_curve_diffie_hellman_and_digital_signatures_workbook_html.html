Note for self-study

The central distinction is this: ECDH creates shared secret material; signatures authenticate messages or transcripts. Modern protocols usually need both.

PartyComputationResult
Alice\(S_A=aB=a(bG)\)\(abG\)

C1 sample answer. Alice applies her scalar to Bob’s public point, and Bob applies his scalar to Alice’s public point. Both operations produce the same scalar multiple of \(G\): \(abG\).

E1 sample answer. ECDH can create shared secret material, but without authentication you may create that secret with an attacker. Authentication binds the exchange to the intended party or trusted identity.

Part F: Static and ephemeral ECDH

TypeMeaningSecurity intuition
Static ECDHLong-term ECDH key pair is reused.Can authenticate identity but gives weaker forward secrecy.
Ephemeral ECDHFresh ECDH key pair is generated for a session.Supports forward secrecy when combined with authentication.
Authenticated ephemeral ECDHFresh ECDH shares are authenticated.Common modern secure-channel pattern.

F1 sample answer. Forward secrecy means that if a long-term key is compromised later, past session keys are not automatically exposed.

Part I: Signing nonce danger

Nonce problemConsequence
Same nonce reused for two messagesPrivate key may be recoverable.
Nonce partially predictablePrivate key may become vulnerable.
Weak random generatorSignatures may leak secrets.
Deterministic nonce generated incorrectlyPrivate key may be exposed.

I1 sample answer. ECDSA’s equations connect the nonce, message hash, signature, and private key. If the nonce is reused or predictable, attackers may solve for the private key.

Part J: EdDSA preview

J1 sample answer. Deterministic nonce generation can reduce reliance on fresh randomness for every signature. That helps avoid failures caused by weak random number generation, though implementations still must be correct.

Part O: Concept checks

O1 sample answer. ECDH is an elliptic-curve key exchange method. Alice publishes \(A=aG\), Bob publishes \(B=bG\), and they compute the same shared point because Alice computes \(aB=a(bG)=abG\) and Bob computes \(bA=b(aG)=abG\).

O2 sample answer. A KDF turns the raw shared material into usable, separated, context-bound traffic keys. Authentication is needed because ECDH alone does not prove that the public point belongs to the intended party.

O3 sample answer. Elliptic-curve digital signatures let a private-key holder sign a message. A verifier uses the public key, the message, and the signature to check validity. ECDSA requires careful nonce handling because nonce reuse can reveal the private key.

O4 sample answer. This tutorial showed how elliptic-curve point multiplication supports both ECDH and digital signatures. ECDH creates shared secret material that should go into a KDF, while signatures authenticate messages and transcripts. Modern protocols often combine ephemeral ECDH, signatures or certificates, KDFs, and symmetric AEAD.
