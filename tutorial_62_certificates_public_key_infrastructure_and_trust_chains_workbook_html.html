How to use this workbook

Treat certificates as signed public-key identity cards. Your goal is to understand what is being bound, who signed it, how the chain is verified, and why name, time, purpose, and revocation checks all matter.

Note for self-study

The central idea is that certificates do not merely contain public keys; they make signed claims about whose public key it is and what that key may be used for.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

QuestionAnswer
A1. What is a certificate?A signed data object that binds a subject/name to a public key and metadata.
A2. What does CA stand for?Certificate Authority.
A3. What is a root CA?A top-level trusted Certificate Authority used as a trust anchor.
A4. What is an intermediate CA?A CA certified by another CA, often used to issue leaf certificates.
A5. What is a leaf certificate?An end-entity certificate, such as a server certificate.
A6. What is the subject of a certificate?The entity the certificate is about.
A7. What is the issuer of a certificate?The CA that signed/issued the certificate.
A8. What does SAN stand for?Subject Alternative Name.
A9. What is a root store?A set of trusted root CA certificates maintained by an operating system, browser, or application.
A10. What is a chain of trust?A sequence of certificates from leaf to intermediate to trusted root.
A11. What is revocation?Distrusting a certificate before its expiration date.
A12. What does OCSP stand for?Online Certificate Status Protocol.

Part E: Chain of trust

Chain answer. Leaf certificate → Intermediate CA → Root CA → Root store trust anchor.

Validation stepWhat the client checks
1The leaf certificate signature verifies under the intermediate CA public key.
2The intermediate certificate signature verifies under the root CA public key.
3The root CA is trusted by the client’s root store.
4The chain is valid for the intended use, time, and name.

E1 sample answer. Intermediate CAs let the root key stay more protected and offline while intermediates perform day-to-day certificate issuance.

Part F: Root stores and trust anchors

QuestionAnswer
Why trust a root CA?Because it is included as a trust anchor in the root store.
Who maintains root stores?Operating systems, browsers, applications, and platform vendors.
Can roots be removed?Yes, root programs can distrust or remove CAs that violate rules.

F1 sample answer. A self-signed certificate is only trusted if the client already trusts it, usually because it is in a root store or was explicitly installed. Signing itself does not create public trust.

Part O: Valid or mistaken?

StatementValid or mistaken?Brief explanation
A certificate binds a public key to a subject/name.ValidThat is the core certificate job.
A CA signs certificates with its private key.ValidThe signature is created with the issuer’s private key.
A root CA is trusted because the certificate signed itself.MistakenIt is trusted because it is in a root store or explicitly trusted.
Hostname validation checks whether the certificate covers the requested site name.ValidThe name is usually checked against the SAN extension.
Revocation means a certificate is distrusted before expiration.ValidIt is an early distrust mechanism.
Certificates usually encrypt all Transport Layer Security application data by themselves.MistakenCertificates authenticate; symmetric traffic keys protect data.

Part P: Concept checks

P1 sample answer. A certificate is a signed statement that binds a public key to a subject or name. It includes metadata such as issuer, validity dates, permitted uses, and names covered. The issuer’s signature lets clients verify that the certificate has not been altered.

P2 sample answer. A trust chain connects a leaf certificate to one or more intermediate certificates and finally to a trusted root in the client’s root store. Each certificate is verified using the public key of its issuer, and the chain must end at a trusted anchor.

P3 sample answer. A browser checks the certificate signature chain, whether the chain ends in a trusted root, whether the hostname matches, whether the certificate is within its validity period, whether the key usage is appropriate, and whether revocation and Certificate Transparency policies are satisfied.

P4 sample answer. This tutorial explained how certificates and Public Key Infrastructure turn raw public keys into trusted identity claims. Certificate authorities sign certificates, clients validate trust chains, and browsers check hostname, time, usage, and status. In modern TLS, certificates authenticate the handshake, while derived symmetric keys protect the data.
