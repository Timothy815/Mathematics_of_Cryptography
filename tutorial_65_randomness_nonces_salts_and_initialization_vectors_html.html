Randomness, Nonces, Salts, and Initialization Vectors

Sorting cryptographic freshness values: what must be secret, what must be unique, what must be unpredictable, what may be public, and what must never be reused.

🏠 Dashboard

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

Earlier tutorials used randomness, salts, nonces, initialization vectors, ephemeral keys, and signature nonces. These terms sound similar, but they have different jobs. This tutorial separates them carefully so the whole cryptographic kitchen stops looking like one giant drawer of mystery spoons.

Cryptography does not merely need secrets. It needs freshness. Sometimes freshness means unpredictability. Sometimes it means uniqueness. Sometimes it means public context. Confusing those requirements is how good mathematics gets ambushed by practical implementation bugs.

What you should know first

You should know the basic roles of encryption, authentication, HMAC, HKDF, AEAD, ECDH, and signatures. The goal here is not to introduce a new primitive but to understand the “extra values” that make primitives safe in real protocols.

The big separation

The first rule is to separate three properties:

PropertyQuestionExample
SecretMust attackers not know it?Encryption key, private key, signing key.
UniqueMust it never repeat in this context?AEAD nonce under the same key.
UnpredictableMust attackers not guess it ahead of time?Cryptographic key, some IVs, signing nonce.
Memory hook

Secret means hidden. Unique means never again. Unpredictable means no one saw it coming.

Randomness and entropy

Cryptographic randomness must be unpredictable to attackers. Random-looking is not enough. A value can look chaotic but be generated by a weak or predictable process.

Definition: entropy

Entropy is uncertainty. In cryptography, high entropy means attackers cannot practically guess the value.

ValueNeeds high entropy?Why?
Symmetric encryption keyYesAttackers must not guess it.
RSA private key primesYesPredictable primes break the private key.
ECDSA signing nonceYesPredictable or reused nonce can reveal the private key.
Password saltUsually should be random/unique, but not secretPrevents shared precomputed attacks across users.

Cryptographically Secure Pseudorandom Number Generators

A CSPRNG takes real entropy and stretches it into many unpredictable-looking bits. Ordinary programming random functions are often not designed for security.

Generator typeUse for cryptography?Reason
Ordinary PRNGNoMay be predictable from seed or outputs.
CSPRNGYesDesigned to resist prediction by attackers.
Practical warning

Do not use casual randomness for keys, private scalars, salts, cryptographic nonces, or signing nonces. Use the platform’s cryptographic random generator.

Nonces: numbers used once

Definition: nonce

A nonce is a value intended to be used once within a specific key and protocol context. In many systems, uniqueness matters more than secrecy.

Nonces are often public. Their job is usually to prevent repeated inputs from producing repeated or dangerous cryptographic behavior.

Use caseNonce requirementFailure mode
AEAD encryptionUsually must be unique per key.Nonce reuse can expose relationships or break authenticity.
Challenge-responseShould be fresh and often unpredictable.Replay attacks.
ECDSA signaturesMust be unique and secret/unpredictable.Private key recovery risk.
Context matters

“Nonce” does not always mean the same security requirement. In AEAD, uniqueness under a key is the central rule. In ECDSA, the signing nonce is much more sensitive.

Nonce reuse disaster

Some encryption modes produce a keystream from a key and nonce. If the same key and nonce are reused, the same keystream may be reused. This can reveal relationships between plaintexts.

\[ C_1=P_1\oplus KS \] \[ C_2=P_2\oplus KS \] \[ C_1\oplus C_2=P_1\oplus P_2 \]

The keystream cancels out. The attacker may not instantly read both messages, but the protective fog thins dramatically. In some authenticated modes, nonce reuse can also break integrity guarantees.

Rule

Never reuse a nonce with the same key in nonce-sensitive encryption modes.

Salts

Definition: salt

A salt is a non-secret value used to diversify hashing or key derivation so identical inputs do not automatically produce identical stored or derived results.

Salts are commonly used in password hashing and key derivation. They do not need to be secret, but they should generally be unique per password or derivation context.

Without saltWith salt
Same password gives same hash.Same password with different salts gives different hashes.
Precomputed attacks are easier to reuse.Attackers must attack each salt context separately.
Duplicate passwords are easier to spot.Duplicate passwords are hidden by different salts.
\[ stored\ value=PasswordHash(password,salt) \]

Initialization Vectors

Definition: Initialization Vector

An Initialization Vector, or IV, is a starting value used by some encryption modes so repeated messages do not begin the same way under the same key.

IV requirements depend on the encryption mode. Some IVs must be unpredictable. Some must be unique. Some systems call their nonce an IV, and some call their IV a nonce. The name is less important than the requirement.

ValueUsually secret?Typical requirement
CBC IVNoUnpredictable for classic security requirements.
CTR nonce/counterNoUnique under the same key.
GCM nonceNoUnique under the same key.
Names are messy

“Nonce” and “IV” are sometimes used inconsistently across libraries and modes. Read the mode requirements, not just the variable name.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: thinking public means unimportant

Salts, nonces, and IVs may be public, but they still have strict requirements.

Mistake 2: reusing nonces under the same key

In many modes, nonce reuse can break confidentiality and sometimes authenticity.

Mistake 3: using ordinary random functions for cryptography

Use a CSPRNG, not a general-purpose pseudo-random function.

Mistake 4: treating salt like a password

Salts do not need to be secret. Their job is diversification, not secrecy.

Mistake 5: assuming IV and nonce always mean the same thing

The required property depends on the mode. Read whether the value must be unique, unpredictable, or both.

Practice problems

  1. What is the difference between secret and unique?
    Show solution

    Secret means attackers must not know it. Unique means it must not repeat in its context.

  2. Does a salt need to be secret?
    Show solution

    No. A salt is usually public, but should generally be unique per password record or derivation context.

  3. What is a nonce?
    Show solution

    A value intended to be used once in a specific key/protocol context.

  4. Why is AEAD nonce reuse dangerous?
    Show solution

    Nonce reuse under the same key can expose relationships between plaintexts and may break authenticity depending on the mode.

  5. What does CSPRNG stand for?
    Show solution

    Cryptographically Secure Pseudorandom Number Generator.

  6. What is the safest habit when a library asks for a nonce or IV?
    Show solution

    Read the specific mode/library requirements and ensure the value meets them, especially uniqueness under the same key.

Why this matters for cryptography

Many cryptographic failures do not come from breaking the main algorithm. They come from using the right algorithm with the wrong freshness value. Reused nonces, weak randomness, predictable signing nonces, missing salts, and misunderstood IVs can collapse systems that look strong on paper.

  • Keys need secrecy and high entropy.
  • Salts are usually public and diversify hashing or derivation.
  • Nonces often need uniqueness under a key.
  • Some values need unpredictability, not merely uniqueness.
  • IV requirements depend on the encryption mode.
  • ECDSA signing nonces are extremely sensitive.
  • CSPRNGs are required for cryptographic randomness.
  • Public freshness values can still be security-critical.
