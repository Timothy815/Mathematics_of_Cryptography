Note for self-study

The central idea is that secrecy, uniqueness, and unpredictability are different properties. Cryptographic values are safe only when they meet the right requirement for their specific role.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

QuestionAnswer
A1. What is randomness in cryptography?Unpredictable bits or values used to generate keys, nonces, salts, and other security-critical values.
A2. What is entropy?Attacker uncertainty; high entropy means the value is hard to guess.
A3. What does CSPRNG stand for?Cryptographically Secure Pseudorandom Number Generator.
A4. What is a nonce?A value intended to be used once in a specific key/protocol context.
A5. What is a salt?A non-secret value used to diversify hashing or key derivation.
A6. What does IV stand for?Initialization Vector.
A7. What does unique mean?It must not repeat in its relevant context.
A8. What does unpredictable mean?An attacker cannot guess it ahead of time.
A9. What does secret mean?Attackers must not know it.
A10. What does public mean?It may be visible without security relying on hiding it.

PropertyQuestionExample

B1 sample answer. Secret means the attacker must not know the value. Unique means it must not repeat in the relevant context. Unpredictable means the attacker cannot guess it before it is generated.

Part C: Randomness and entropy

ValueNeeds high entropy?Why?
Symmetric encryption keyYesAttackers must not guess it.
RSA private key primesYesPredictable primes break the private key.

C1 sample answer. A value can appear random but still be generated by a predictable process. Cryptographic security requires unpredictability to attackers, not just visual messiness.

Part D: CSPRNG versus ordinary PRNG

Generator typeUse for cryptography?Reason
Ordinary PRNGNoMay be predictable from seed or outputs.

D1 sample answer. General-purpose random functions are usually designed for simulation or convenience, not attacker resistance. Cryptographic values require a CSPRNG.

Part E: Nonces

Use caseNonce requirementFailure mode
AEAD encryptionUsually must be unique per key.Nonce reuse can expose relationships or break authenticity.
Challenge-responseShould be fresh and often unpredictable.Replay attacks.

E1 sample answer. Different protocols use nonces for different security goals. In one mode the main rule may be uniqueness, while in another setting the nonce must also be secret or unpredictable.

Part F: Nonce reuse algebra

F1 sample answer. XORing the ciphertexts gives \((P_1\oplus KS)\oplus(P_2\oplus KS)\). Since \(KS\oplus KS=0\), the keystream cancels, leaving \(P_1\oplus P_2\).

F2 sample answer. It does not always instantly reveal both plaintexts, but it reveals a relationship between them. With guesses, known structure, or one known plaintext, the attacker can recover more information.

Part G: Salts

Without saltWith salt
Same password gives same hash.Same password with different salts gives different hashes.
Precomputed attacks are easier to reuse.Attackers must attack each salt context separately.
Duplicate passwords are easier to spot.Duplicate passwords are hidden by different salts.

G1 sample answer. A salt’s job is diversification, not secrecy. It prevents identical inputs from producing identical results and makes precomputed attacks less reusable.

Part L: Concept checks

L1 sample answer. Randomness is about producing values attackers cannot predict. Uniqueness means a value does not repeat in its required context. Secrecy means attackers must not know the value. A value can be public but still need uniqueness, such as an AEAD nonce.

L2 sample answer. Nonce reuse can be disastrous because many modes derive keystream or authentication behavior from the key and nonce. Reusing the same nonce with the same key can reveal relationships between plaintexts and sometimes break authenticity.

L3 sample answer. A salt is a usually public value that diversifies password hashing or key derivation, making identical inputs produce different outputs. An IV is a starting value for an encryption mode, and its requirements depend on the mode, such as uniqueness or unpredictability.

L4 sample answer. This tutorial separated secret, unique, unpredictable, and public properties. It explained entropy, CSPRNGs, nonces, salts, IVs, nonce reuse, and the importance of reading each cryptographic mode’s exact freshness requirements.
