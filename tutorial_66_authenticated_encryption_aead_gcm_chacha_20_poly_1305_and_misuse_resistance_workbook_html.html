Note for self-study

The central AEAD idea is simple but strict: do not trust ciphertext, metadata, or plaintext unless the authentication tag verifies under the correct key, nonce, and associated data.

Part B: Why encryption alone is not enough

GoalEncryption aloneAuthenticated encryption
ConfidentialityYes, if used correctly.Yes, if used correctly.
IntegrityNot necessarily.Yes.
AuthenticityNot necessarily.Yes, under the shared key.

B1 sample answer. Some encryption modes are malleable, meaning attackers may modify ciphertext and affect the decrypted plaintext. Authentication detects tampering before plaintext is trusted.

G1 sample answer. GCM nonce reuse can reuse keystream and can also damage the authentication guarantees, potentially compromising both confidentiality and integrity.

Part H: GCM math shape

H1 answer. \(C=P\oplus KS\) represents counter-mode confidentiality. \(tag=GHASH_H(AAD,C)\oplus mask\) represents authentication over AAD and ciphertext.

Part K: AEAD nonce requirements

Nonce behaviorSecurity result
Unique per keyExpected safe use.
Repeated with same keyDangerous; may break confidentiality and/or authenticity.
Public but uniqueUsually acceptable.
Secret but repeatedStill dangerous. Secrecy does not repair reuse.

K1 sample answer. AEAD nonce rules depend on uniqueness under the key. Hiding a repeated nonce does not prevent the cryptographic relationship created by reuse.

Part L: Misuse resistance

Mode typeIf nonce is reusedLesson
Nonce-sensitive AEADMay suffer severe security failure.Nonce management must be correct.

L1 sample answer. Misuse resistance means a mode reduces damage from some implementation mistakes, such as accidental nonce reuse. It does not mean the mode is invincible or that correct nonce/key rules no longer matter.

Part P: Concept checks

P1 sample answer. AEAD is authenticated encryption with associated data. It encrypts plaintext, authenticates ciphertext, and authenticates public associated data. It outputs ciphertext and a tag, and decryption should only release plaintext if the tag verifies.

P2 sample answer. AAD is public metadata that is not encrypted but is included in authentication. Tag verification checks whether the ciphertext, AAD, nonce, and key match the expected values. If verification fails, the message should be rejected.

P3 sample answer. AES-GCM uses AES in counter mode for encryption and GHASH for authentication. ChaCha20-Poly1305 uses the ChaCha20 stream cipher for encryption and Poly1305 for authentication. Both are widely used AEAD constructions and require unique nonces under the same key.

P4 sample answer. This tutorial explained why encryption alone is not enough and how AEAD combines confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity. It covered associated data, tags, AES-GCM, ChaCha20-Poly1305, nonce requirements, misuse resistance, and safe implementation patterns.
