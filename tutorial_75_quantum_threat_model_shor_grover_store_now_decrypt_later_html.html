Quantum Threat Model: Shor, Grover, and Store-Now-Decrypt-Later

A practical threat-modeling lesson for post-quantum cryptography: what quantum attackers change, what they do not change, how Shor differs from Grover, why public-key cryptography is under special pressure, and how long-lived secrets create migration urgency before large quantum computers exist.

🏠 Dashboard

Why this tutorial exists

Purpose

Tutorial 74 gave the broad post-quantum map. This tutorial sharpens the threat model. Not every cryptographic tool is affected equally by quantum computing. Some public-key systems face a cliff. Symmetric systems face a slope. Long-lived secrets create urgency even before the cliff is visible from the road.

The quantum threat is not a cartoon ray gun pointed equally at all cryptography. It is more like a specialized set of skeleton keys. Some locks fall open. Some merely need thicker metal. Some are not the target at all.

What you should know first

You should know RSA, Diffie-Hellman, elliptic-curve cryptography, symmetric key search, hash functions, bits of security, key exchange, signatures, and the post-quantum family map from Tutorial 74.

The core quantum threat model

A quantum threat model asks: what if an attacker eventually has access to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer? The immediate question is not only “Can they break traffic in the future?” but also “Can they record traffic today that must remain secret long enough to be worth breaking later?”

The attacker does not need tomorrow’s crowbar to steal today’s locked box. They only need shelf space.

Shor’s algorithm: the public-key cliff

Shor’s algorithm is dangerous because it efficiently solves the mathematical problems behind common classical public-key systems: factoring and discrete logarithms.

SystemHard problemShor impact
RSA encryption/signaturesFactoring \(N=pq\)Breaks the trapdoor assumption.
Finite-field Diffie-HellmanDiscrete logarithmBreaks key exchange.
Elliptic-curve Diffie-HellmanElliptic-curve discrete logarithmBreaks key exchange.
ECDSA / EdDSA-style signaturesElliptic-curve discrete logarithmBreaks signature security.
Important distinction

Increasing RSA key sizes or elliptic-curve sizes is not the same kind of answer as increasing symmetric key sizes. Against Shor’s algorithm, the underlying mathematical family itself is the problem.

Grover’s algorithm: the search slope

Grover’s algorithm gives a quadratic speedup for unstructured search. In rough cryptographic planning, an ideal \(n\)-bit brute-force search moves from about \(2^n\) classical work toward about \(2^{n/2}\) quantum search work.

\[ classical\ search\approx 2^n \] \[ Grover\text{-style search}\approx 2^{n/2} \]
Symmetric key sizeClassical brute forceGrover-style estimatePlanning lesson
128 bits\(2^{128}\)\(2^{64}\)May be too small for high-value quantum-era margin.
192 bits\(2^{192}\)\(2^{96}\)Stronger, but still below 128-bit quantum-search margin.
256 bits\(2^{256}\)\(2^{128}\)Common conservative quantum-era symmetric target.

Grover does not make symmetric cryptography obsolete. It changes security margins.

Quantum effects on hashes

Hash functions have several security goals, and quantum attacks affect them differently. The simple planning lesson is to choose digest lengths with enough margin for the intended goal.

Hash goalClassical rough work for \(n\)-bit digestQuantum-era caution
Preimage resistanceAbout \(2^n\)Grover-like speedup suggests larger outputs for high security.
Second-preimage resistanceAbout \(2^n\)Use adequate digest length and modern construction.
Collision resistanceAbout \(2^{n/2}\)Collision security already loses half the exponent classically.
Do not over-flatten the story

Quantum hash analysis is more nuanced than “everything is cut in half.” For this stage, the important lesson is that hash output sizes and security goals must be chosen with quantum-era margins in mind.

What quantum computers do not automatically break

Quantum computers do not automatically break every use of cryptography. A threat model must distinguish families and assumptions.

Cryptographic areaQuantum-era status
AES-style symmetric encryptionStill usable with sufficient key sizes and safe modes.
Modern hashesStill usable with appropriate output sizes and goals.
HMAC and KDFsStill important, with adequate underlying hash/security margins.
RSA / DH / ECC public-key systemsNeed replacement or hybrid transition.
ProtocolsNeed migration, transcript binding, downgrade defense, and crypto-agility.

The migration timing problem

A useful way to reason about urgency is to compare three time periods:

SymbolMeaning
\(X\)Security lifetime: how long the data must remain secret.
\(Y\)Migration time: how long it takes to deploy quantum-resistant protection.
\(Z\)Time until a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists.
\[ X + Y > Z \quad \Rightarrow \quad migration\ is\ urgent \]

This is sometimes called Mosca’s inequality in discussions of quantum migration. The exact dates may be uncertain, but the logic is sharp: if secrets need to last longer than the time remaining after migration delay, waiting is dangerous.

Hybrid threat-model logic

A hybrid key exchange combines classical and post-quantum contributions. A common design goal is that the final secret remains protected if at least one component remains secure.

\[ shared\_secret = KDF(classical\_secret \parallel pqc\_secret \parallel transcript) \]
If classical part fails...If PQC part holds...Hybrid result
Quantum attacker breaks ECDH.ML-KEM-style secret remains secure.Final secret can remain protected.
Classical part remains secure.PQC assumption later weakens.Final secret may still have classical protection.
Both parts fail.No secure contribution remains.Hybrid protection fails.
Hybrid warning

Hybrid designs must still prevent downgrade. If an attacker can strip out the post-quantum part or force a weak mode, the hybrid design becomes decorative armor.

Signatures have a different timeline problem

Key exchange protects confidentiality of sessions. Signatures protect authenticity and integrity of messages, certificates, software updates, logs, and legal or archival records. Migrating key exchange alone does not migrate signatures.

Signature useQuantum-era concern
Software updatesForged updates could compromise systems.
CertificatesCertificate chains must move away from vulnerable signature algorithms.
Documents and recordsLong-term validation may need timestamps and PQC signatures.
Firmware imagesEmbedded devices may trust old signature schemes for many years.
Authentication tokensForged signatures can impersonate authority.

Common mistakes

Mistake 1: treating quantum risk as all-or-nothing

Quantum attacks affect different primitives differently. The threat model must be specific.

Mistake 2: thinking symmetric crypto is dead

Symmetric cryptography remains usable with appropriate key sizes and designs.

Mistake 3: migrating key exchange but forgetting signatures

A system may still depend on RSA, ECDSA, or EdDSA signatures in certificates, firmware, or updates.

Mistake 4: ignoring data lifetime

Data that must remain confidential for decades creates urgency today.

Mistake 5: assuming hybrid automatically means secure

Hybrid protocols still need transcript binding, downgrade protection, correct KDF design, and clean error handling.

Practice problems

  1. What is a cryptographically relevant quantum computer?
    Show solution

    A fault-tolerant quantum computer capable of running attacks against deployed cryptographic parameters.

  2. Which common public-key systems are threatened by Shor’s algorithm?
    Show solution

    RSA, finite-field Diffie-Hellman, elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman, and elliptic-curve signature systems such as ECDSA/EdDSA-style systems.

  3. Why does Grover’s algorithm not destroy symmetric cryptography in the same way?
    Show solution

    It gives a quadratic search speedup, which can often be addressed by using larger symmetric keys, rather than breaking the underlying family outright.

  4. What is store-now, decrypt-later?
    Show solution

    An attacker records encrypted data now and waits to decrypt it later when stronger attacks or quantum computers become available.

  5. What does \(X+Y>Z\) mean in migration planning?
    Show solution

    If data lifetime plus migration time exceeds the time until a cryptographically relevant quantum computer, migration is urgent.

  6. Why is migrating signatures separate from migrating key exchange?
    Show solution

    Key exchange establishes secrets; signatures authenticate data, certificates, updates, and identities. They solve different protocol jobs.

Why this matters for cryptography

A good quantum threat model prevents panic and complacency at the same time. It tells us which systems face a cliff, which systems need larger margins, which secrets are urgent because of long lifetimes, and which protocol components need migration. It turns “quantum someday” into concrete engineering questions.

  • Shor threatens factoring and discrete-log public-key systems.
  • Grover weakens brute-force margins but does not erase symmetric cryptography.
  • Hash goals need quantum-era security margins.
  • Store-now, decrypt-later makes future attacks relevant today.
  • Migration urgency depends on data lifetime, migration time, and quantum arrival estimates.
  • Hybrid protocols can reduce transition risk when designed carefully.
  • Signatures and key exchange must be migrated separately.
  • Crypto-agility is a survival trait.
