\[ X + Y > Z \quad \Rightarrow \quad migration\ is\ urgent \]

\(X\): security lifetime, \(Y\): migration time, \(Z\): time until a cryptographically relevant quantum computer.

Note for self-study

The central idea is that quantum risk is specific, not foggy. Shor breaks major public-key assumptions, Grover changes search margins, and store-now-decrypt-later makes future capability relevant today.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

QuestionAnswer
A1. What is a threat model?An explicit description of attacker capabilities, goals, access, and time horizon.
A2. What is a cryptographically relevant quantum computer, or CRQC?A fault-tolerant quantum computer capable of attacking deployed cryptographic parameters.
A3. What is Shor’s algorithm?A quantum algorithm that efficiently solves factoring and discrete logarithms.
A4. What is Grover’s algorithm?A quantum algorithm giving a quadratic speedup for unstructured search.
A5. What is store-now, decrypt-later?An attack strategy where encrypted data is recorded now and decrypted later when stronger attacks exist.
A6. What is harvest now, decrypt later, or HNDL?Another name for recording encrypted data now for future decryption.
A7. What is forward secrecy?A property where past session keys remain protected even if long-term keys are later compromised.
A8. What is security lifetime?How long data must remain confidential.
A9. What is migration time?The time needed to inventory, upgrade, deploy, test, and retire vulnerable cryptography.
A10. What is crypto-agility?The ability to safely upgrade cryptographic algorithms, parameters, and protocols.

Part B: Shor’s algorithm

SystemHard problemShor impact
RSA encryption/signaturesFactoring \(N=pq\)Breaks the trapdoor assumption.
Finite-field Diffie-HellmanDiscrete logarithmBreaks key exchange.
Elliptic-curve Diffie-HellmanElliptic-curve discrete logarithmBreaks key exchange.

B1 sample answer. Shor attacks the mathematical assumptions behind RSA and elliptic curves directly. Larger parameters do not provide the same simple margin increase that larger symmetric keys provide against Grover-style search.

Part D: Quantum effects on hashes

Hash goalClassical rough work for \(n\)-bit digestQuantum-era caution
Preimage resistanceAbout \(2^n\)Grover-like speedup suggests larger outputs for high security.
Second-preimage resistanceAbout \(2^n\)Use adequate digest length and modern construction.
Collision resistanceAbout \(2^{n/2}\)Collision security already loses half the exponent classically.

D1 sample answer. Hash functions have different security goals, and quantum effects vary by goal. Collision resistance already has a birthday-bound reduction classically, so the story is more nuanced than one universal halving rule.

Part M: Concept checks

M1 sample answer. A quantum threat model identifies what a future quantum-capable attacker can do, when they may be able to do it, what traffic they can record today, and what data or signatures must remain secure long enough to matter.

M2 sample answer. Shor’s algorithm threatens the core assumptions behind RSA, Diffie-Hellman, elliptic-curve key exchange, and elliptic-curve signatures. Grover’s algorithm speeds up brute-force search, changing symmetric security margins rather than destroying symmetric cryptography outright.

M3 sample answer. Store-now, decrypt-later means attackers record ciphertext today and decrypt it later if future quantum machines break the key exchange. \(X+Y>Z\) means the data lifetime plus migration time exceeds the estimated time until a cryptographically relevant quantum computer, so migration is urgent.

M4 sample answer. Key exchange establishes session secrets, while signatures authenticate certificates, software updates, firmware, identities, and records. A system can migrate one and still remain vulnerable through the other.
