Part F: Shortest vector thinking

F1. The zero vector is always length 0, so including it would make the problem trivial. We exclude it to find meaningful lattice structure.

F2. A short nonzero vector may reveal hidden relationships among the basis vectors or secret structure encoded in the lattice.

G2. Without noise, b=5·3=15pmod{17}.

G3. The noisy result no longer exactly matches a· s, so an attacker cannot directly solve the clean equation for s.

Part I: Concept explanations

I1 sample. Basis vectors generate a lattice by allowing whole-number multiples of each vector to be added together. Every such integer combination is a lattice point.

I2 sample. A good basis has short, nearly perpendicular vectors, making nearby lattice points easier to find. A bad basis uses long, slanted, tangled vectors, hiding the lattice’s structure.

I3 sample. Two-dimensional pictures help us understand the idea, but cryptographic lattices live in high dimensions where there are too many directions and candidates to visualize directly.

I4 sample. Noise is useful because it prevents equations from being solved exactly with ordinary linear algebra. The secret remains hidden behind small intentional errors.
