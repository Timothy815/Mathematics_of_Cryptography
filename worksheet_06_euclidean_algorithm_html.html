How to use this worksheet

The Euclidean algorithm is a repeated division process. At each step, divide the larger number by the smaller, record the remainder, then replace the pair with the divisor and remainder. Stop when the remainder is zero. The answer is the last nonzero remainder.

Note for self-study

Always check that each remainder is smaller than the previous divisor. The answer is the last nonzero remainder, not the zero remainder itself.

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

A1. \(\gcd(a,b)\) is the largest number that divides both \(a\) and \(b\) evenly.

A2. \(q\) is the quotient (how many full copies fit); \(r\) is the remainder (what is left over).

A3. The next pair is \((b,r)\).

A4. \(\gcd(a,n)=1\) means \(a\) and \(n\) are coprime — exactly the condition for \(a\) to have a multiplicative inverse modulo \(n\).

Part G: Explain the process

G1 sample. Because \(r=a-qb\), any common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) also divides \(r\). Replacing with \((a,r)\) would break the relationship — we need the divisor \(b\) as the new larger number.

G2 sample. When the remainder reaches zero, the current divisor divides everything remaining in the chain. Working backwards, it divides every step, so it divides the original pair. It is the largest such divisor.

G3 sample. The Euclidean algorithm finds the greatest common divisor. The extended version also finds coefficients \(s,t\) such that \(sa+tb=\gcd(a,b)\), which gives modular inverses when \(\gcd(a,n)=1\).

Part H: Challenge problems

H1. \(\gcd(987,610)=1\). Consecutive Fibonacci numbers are always coprime — each is the previous two added together, and any common divisor would have to divide their difference, which is the prior Fibonacci number, leading to a chain that terminates at 1.

H2. \(4096=16\cdot255+16\), \(255=15\cdot16+15\), \(16=1\cdot15+1\), \(15=15\cdot1+0\). \(\gcd(4096,255)=1\). Yes, 255 has an inverse modulo 4096.
