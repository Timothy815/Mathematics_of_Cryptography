Directions

How to use this worksheet

Work in two phases. First, run the Euclidean algorithm forward to find the greatest common divisor. Second, walk backward through the remainder equations, substituting one equation into the next, until only the original two numbers remain. The coefficient of the number you are inverting is the modular inverse — adjust it to a positive residue if it is negative by adding the modulus.

Note for self-study

Always read the coefficient of the number you are inverting — not the coefficient of the modulus. If that coefficient is negative, add the modulus once. Check your answer: multiply \(a\) by the inverse and confirm the product is \(\equiv1\pmod{n}\).

Part A: Warm-up vocabulary

A1. Bézout's identity says that for any integers \(a\) and \(b\), there exist integers \(s\) and \(t\) such that \(\gcd(a,b)=sa+tb\). The greatest common divisor is always expressible as an integer combination of the two numbers.

A2. Bézout coefficients are the integers \(s\) and \(t\) that satisfy \(\gcd(a,b)=sa+tb\).

A3. If \(1=sa+tn\), then reducing modulo \(n\) makes the \(tn\) term vanish (since \(n\equiv0\pmod{n}\)), leaving \(1\equiv sa\pmod{n}\). So \(s\) is the inverse of \(a\) modulo \(n\).

A4. Add the modulus \(n\) to the negative coefficient until the result falls in \([0,n)\). One addition is usually enough.

Part B: Forward Euclidean steps

B1. \(\gcd(26,15)\):

\[26=1\cdot15+11\quad 15=1\cdot11+4\quad 11=2\cdot4+3\quad 4=1\cdot3+1\quad 3=3\cdot1+0\]

\(\gcd(26,15)=1\).

B2. \(\gcd(151,25)\):

\[151=6\cdot25+1\quad 25=25\cdot1+0\]

\(\gcd(151,25)=1\).

B3. \(\gcd(64,35)\):

\[64=1\cdot35+29\quad 35=1\cdot29+6\quad 29=4\cdot6+5\quad 6=1\cdot5+1\quad 5=5\cdot1+0\]

\(\gcd(64,35)=1\).

Part C: Guided back-substitution

C1.

\[1=4-3\] \[1=4-(11-2\cdot4)=3\cdot4-11\] \[1=3(15-11)-11=3\cdot15-4\cdot11\] \[1=3\cdot15-4(26-15)=7\cdot15-4\cdot26\]

So \(15^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{26}\).

C2. Rearranging \(151=6\cdot25+1\): \(1=151-6\cdot25\). Coefficient of 25 is \(-6\). Since \(-6+151=145\), we get \(25^{-1}\equiv145\pmod{151}\).

Part D: Find modular inverses

D1. \(12=1\cdot7+5\), \(7=1\cdot5+2\), \(5=2\cdot2+1\).

\[1=5-2\cdot2=5-2(7-5)=3\cdot5-2\cdot7=3(12-7)-2\cdot7=3\cdot12-5\cdot7\]

Coefficient of 7 is \(-5\). \(-5+12=7\). So \(7^{-1}\equiv7\pmod{12}\).

D2. \(26=2\cdot11+4\), \(11=2\cdot4+3\), \(4=1\cdot3+1\).

\[1=4-3=4-(11-2\cdot4)=3\cdot4-11=3(26-2\cdot11)-11=3\cdot26-7\cdot11\]

Coefficient of 11 is \(-7\). \(-7+26=19\). So \(11^{-1}\equiv19\pmod{26}\).

D3. \(64=1\cdot35+29\), \(35=1\cdot29+6\), \(29=4\cdot6+5\), \(6=1\cdot5+1\).

\[1=6-5=6-(29-4\cdot6)=5\cdot6-29=5(35-29)-29=5\cdot35-6\cdot29\] \[1=5\cdot35-6(64-35)=11\cdot35-6\cdot64\]

Coefficient of 35 is \(11\). So \(35^{-1}\equiv11\pmod{64}\).

Part G: Affine cipher decryption

G1. \(P\equiv7(13-8)=7\cdot5=35\equiv9\pmod{26}\).

G2. \(P\equiv19(20-2)=19\cdot18=342\equiv4\pmod{26}\). (342 = 13·26 + 4.)

G3 sample answer. \(\gcd(13,26)=13\neq1\), so 13 has no inverse modulo 26. Every output of \(13P\bmod26\) is a multiple of 13, so only 0 and 13 can appear — the cipher cannot encrypt all 26 letters, making it impossible to recover a unique plaintext from a ciphertext.

Part H: Challenge problem

H1. Forward steps: \(3120=183\cdot17+9\), \(17=1\cdot9+8\), \(9=1\cdot8+1\).

Back-substitution:

\[1=9-8=9-(17-9)=2\cdot9-17=2(3120-183\cdot17)-17=2\cdot3120-367\cdot17\]

Coefficient of 17 is \(-367\). \(-367+3120=2753\). So \(17^{-1}\equiv2753\pmod{3120}\).

Check: \(17\times2753=46801=15\times3120+1\). \(\checkmark\)
