Answer Key

Note: Written responses are not graded automatically — compare your explanations to the sample answers below.

Part A

A1. A modulus polynomial is the polynomial used as the reduction rule — analogous to the modulus in integer modular arithmetic.

A2. Polynomial multiplication can produce powers higher than \(x^7\); reduction folds those high powers back into byte-sized (degree ≤ 7) form.

A3. An irreducible polynomial cannot be written as a product of two lower-degree nonconstant polynomials over the same field.

A4. 8

A5. prime (prime number)

Part B

Polynomial · Polynomial (modulus polynomial) · Polynomial remainder · Irreducible polynomial ensures every nonzero element has an inverse

Part C

C1. \(x^3\equiv x+1\) (from \(x^3+x+1\equiv0 \Rightarrow x^3=x+1\))

C2. \(x^4=x\cdot x^3\equiv x(x+1)=x^2+x\)

C3. \(x^5=x^2\cdot x^3\equiv x^2(x+1)=x^3+x^2\equiv(x+1)+x^2=x^2+x+1\)

Part D

D1. \(x^3+x^2+1\equiv(x+1)+x^2+1=x^2+x\)

D2. \(x^4+x^2\equiv(x^2+x)+x^2=x\)

D3. \(x^5+x^2+x\equiv(x^2+x+1)+x^2+x=1\)

D4. \(x^4+x^3+1\equiv(x^2+x)+(x+1)+1=x^2+(x+x)+(1+1)=x^2\)

Part E

E1. \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\)

E2. 0x11B

E3. \(x^4+x^3+x+1\)

E4. 0x1B

E5. The term \(x^8\) corresponds to bit position 8 (the 9th bit), so the full polynomial cannot be stored in an 8-bit byte.

Part F

F1.

\[x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\equiv0\implies x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1\]

F2. Binary: 00011011 · Hex: 0x1B

Part G

G1. \(x^8\equiv x^4+x^3+x+1\)

G2. \((x^4+x^3+x+1)+x^4+x^2=x^3+x^2+x+1\)

G3. \((x^4+x^3+x+1)+x^7+x^4+x+1=x^7+x^3\)

G4. \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\equiv0\) (the modulus reduces to zero mod itself)

Part H

H1 sample. Primes cannot be factored in the integers (except by 1 and themselves). Irreducible polynomials cannot be factored into lower-degree pieces within the field. Both guarantee that every nonzero element in the resulting structure has a multiplicative inverse.

H2 sample. An irreducible modulus ensures the 256 byte-polynomials form a field \(\mathrm{GF}(2^8)\). In a field, every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse, which is required for the AES S-box.

H3. False — only irreducible polynomials produce a field. A reducible modulus would give a ring with zero divisors where some nonzero bytes have no inverse, breaking the S-box construction.
